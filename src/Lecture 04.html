Discrete Probability

How many times must I flip a "fair" coin to get $P(H)=0.5$

As the number of trials in an experiment increases, the average of the results gets closer to the expected value (recall LLN). For a "fair" coin, this means that with enough flips, the proportion of heads ($P(H)$) approaches 0.5.

Assumptions:

  • Each flip is independent of previous flips.
  • The probability of heads, P(H), is constant, or identical at 0.5 for each flip.
  • The coin is "fair" $\therefore P(H) = P(T) \Rightarrow 1=P(H \cup T)=2P(H)\, \text{or}\, P(H) = \frac{1}{2}$

The Strong Law of Large Numbers ensures that $E[P(X=H)]=\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = \mu $.

In the case of the "fair" coin, $\mu = 0.5$.

That is the expected probability is $E[P(H)] = 0.5$ for a $H$ in each flip.

So, what's the problem?

This is impractical in experimental design, your CEO or finance department will laugh in your face if you ask for, near infinity samples.

Binomial Distribution

Application in Mechanical Engineering

The binomial distribution is pivotal in mechanical engineering for predicting the reliability and failure rates of components. Understanding the distribution helps engineers design more reliable systems by estimating the likelihood of multiple failures and ensuring that systems can operate effectively under a range of conditions.

For instance, in quality control processes, the binomial distribution can predict the number of defective parts in a batch, aiding in the decision-making process for quality assurance and control.

Mathematical Foundation

The binomial distribution is defined by two parameters: n and p. Here, n represents the number of trials, and p is the probability of success on a single trial.

The probability of getting exactly k successes in n trials is given by the formula:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

This formula helps calculate the likelihood of a specific number of successes, which is crucial for risk assessment and planning in engineering projects.

Binomial Distribution

Scenario: a coin is flipped 28 times, resulting in 19 heads.

If the coin is "fair", how likely is this

  • $P(X=k)$: probability of exactly $k$ successes in $n$ trials.
  • $\binom{n}{k}$: the number of ways to choose $k$ successes out of $n$ trials. $\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$
  • $p$: probability of success in a trial.
  • $1-p$: probability of failure in a trial (compliment).
  • $n$: total number of trials.
  • $k$: number of successes tested.

Using the binomial distribution formula:

$P(X = 19) = \binom{28}{19} (0.5)^{19} (0.5)^{9}=\class{info}{0.0257}$

Poisson Distribution

Definition: $P(X=k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$

Where:

  • $\lambda$ is the average rate (events per interval)
  • $k$ is the number of occurrences
  • $e$ is the base of the natural logarithm

This formula gives the probability of observing $k$ events in a fixed interval of time or space, given the events occur with a known constant mean rate and independently of the time since the last event.

The Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution that expresses the probability of a given number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space if these events occur with a known constant rate and independently of the time since the last event.

In mechanical engineering, the Poisson distribution is important for predicting the occurrence of rare events over a continuous period, such as system failures, arrival of tasks in queueing theory, or the number of flaws found in a material. Understanding and applying the Poisson distribution allows engineers to design more reliable systems, schedule maintenance more effectively, and manage resources efficiently.

Poisson Distribution

Scenario: A factory produces metal rods, and historically, defects occur at an average rate of 2 defects per 10 meters ($\lambda = 2$). In a randomly sampled 10-meter segment, 5 defects are observed.

What is the probability of observing 5 or more defects in a 10-meter segment?

  • $P(X=k)$: probability of observing exactly $k$ defects.
  • $\lambda$: the average rate of defects per interval.
  • $e$: the base of the natural logarithm.
  • $k!$: factorial of $k$.

Using the Poisson distribution formula:

$P(X = 5) = \frac{2^5 e^{-2}}{5!} = \class{info}{0.0361}$

Calculate the p-value:

The p-value is the probability of observing 5 or more defects:

$P(X \geq 5) = \sum_{x=5}^{\infty} \frac{2^x e^{-2}}{x!}$ $= \class{danger}{0.0527}$

Poisson Analysis Results

  • Expected Defects per 10m: 2
  • Observed Defects: 5
  • Observed Probability: 0.0361
  • P-Value: 0.0527

The Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of times an event occurs within a fixed interval of time or space. This distribution is particularly useful for modeling events that happen independently and at a constant average rate.

The Normal Distribution

The normal (or Gaussian) distribution is a continuous probability distribution characterized by a symmetric, bell-shaped curve.

PDF:

$$f(\class{warning}{x}; \class{info}{\mu}, \class{info}{\sigma}) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$$
  • $\mu$ = Central Tendency
  • 68% of the observations lie within 1 standard "distance" of the center
  • $\sigma$ = Spread (like width)
  • 95% lie within 1.96 standard "distance" of the central tendency
  • $x$ = Specific value of the continuous variable
  • 99% lie within 2.58 standard "distance" of the central tendency

Often denoted $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the normal distribution is special as it underscores the Central Limit Theorem's revelation that sums of independent variables universally converge to this form, regardless of their initial distributions.

The normal distribution stands as a fundamental pillar in statistics, shaping our understanding of natural phenomena, from distributions of heights to test score with its iconic "bell curve".

Central Limit Theorem

In the 18th century, a few mathematicians, abraham de moivre, and later Pierre-Simon Laplace then Carl Gauss and others, observed that if you looked at the averages of many samples, the distribution of those averages converged on the normal distribution. Even when the samples were taken from a underlying distribution which was not normally shaped.

Adjust parameters, run simulations.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the distribution of the sum (or average) of a large number of independent, identically distributed random variables, each with a finite mean and variance, will approximate a normal distribution, regardless of the original distribution of the variables. This convergence towards normality occurs as the sample size increases, underscoring the normal distribution's significance in probability and statistics.

Normally Distributed Data

Example: In a mechanical system, the stress endurance limit of a material is normally distributed with a mean ($\mu$) of 300 MPa and a standard deviation ($\sigma$) of 50 MPa.

What's the probability of selecting a sample with a stress endurance limit at least as great as 350 MPa?

Calculation:

$P(X > 350) = 1 - P(X \leq 350) = 1 - \int_{-\infty}^{350} f(x)\,dx$

Where $f(x)\,dx$ is the PDF ($f(x;\mu,\, \sigma)$), then $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1$ is the cumulative density function.

Cumulative Density Function

A Cumulative Density Function (CDF) is a function that indicates the probability that a random variable takes on a value less than or equal to a given point. It's a way to describe the distribution of random variables, providing a complete picture of the probability distribution.

The CDF starts at 0 and increases to 1 as you move along the x-axis, with the rate of increase depending on the distribution of the variable. It gives insights into the percentiles and probabilities of the underlying distribution, making it a fundamental tool in statistics and probability theory.

Definitions

Random Variable

A function that assigns a real number to each outcome in the probability space

Discrete Variable

Random variable that measures outcomes at distinct or finite points

Continuous Variable

Random variable that measures outcomes at any value within a range of real numbers

Probability Distribution

A statistical function that describes all the possible values and likelihoods that a random variable can take within a given range

Discrete Probability Distribution

Used for discrete random variables. The probability of each possible outcome is specified by a probability mass function (PMF), with the probability of an outcome being exactly one ($x$) of the set ($S$) is calculated as $\sum_{x\in S}P(X=x)$

Continuous Probability Distribution

Applies to continuous random variables. The probabilities of specific outcomes are described by a probability density function (PDF), with the probability of an outcome within a certain range ($[a,\, b]$) calculated as the area under the curve: $P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx$

Note that we say statistical function, this mostly convention for statisticians and mathematicians to produce "proofs" and publish papers. In the real world, these functions are used as models to describe the probability behavior of the data.
