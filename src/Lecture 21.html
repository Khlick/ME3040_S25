$\chi^2$ Goodness Of Fit

Fitting Distributions

How did we assess frequency tables with more than 2 levels in a category?

Chi-Squared ($\chi^2$)

A statistic that is widely used to describe the difference in distribution patterns of categorical variable(s) is the square of the relative differences of each observed frequencies compared to the expected frequencies. This is known as the Chi-Squared statistic for comparing frequencies of a categorical variable.

$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{(\mathrm{Observed}_i-\mathrm{Expected}_i)^2}{\mathrm{Expected}_i}$

$\chi^2$ summarizes the discrepancy between observed and expected frequencies:

  • The smaller the overall discrepancy between the observed and expected scores, the smaller the value of the $\chi^2$.
  • Conversely, the larger the discrepancy between the observed and expected scores, the larger the value of the $\chi^2$.

Recall, chi-squared is the statistic we use to sum up the relative differences in COUNTS from corresponding bins of 2 distributions.

Differences Between Bins

Calculating a \( \chi^2 \) statistic is similar to determining normalized squared residuals in a linear regression model, applied to categorical data in histograms. It assesses deviations of observed categories from expected outcomes under a hypothesis.

How significantly does the distribution pattern of observed frequencies differ from the theoretical distribution? This analysis mirrors the evaluation of sum of squares between groups in ANOVA, which measures variance due to different groupings within the data.

Key idea

When we calculate a \( \chi^2 \) statistic to compare distribution patterns of categorical variables, this is called a \( \chi^2 \) goodness of fit test.

Note that here we compare our data to a theoretical distribution, when else did we do that?

$\chi^2$ Goodness Of Fit

When calculating the \( \chi^2 \) statistic to assess the compatibility of observed with expected distribution frequencies of categorical variables, the procedure is termed a \( \chi^2 \) goodness of fit test.

$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{(\mathrm{Observed}_i-\mathrm{Expected}_i)^2}{\mathrm{Expected}_i}$

Follow Up

We found a difference in distribution pattern that may be considered statistically significant.

So, what's next

The $\chi^2$ test tells you whether you have an overall difference between the observed and expected pattern, but it does not tell you which specific groups differ: post hoc tests do.

Note: Because post hoc tests are run to confirm where the differences occurred between groups, they should only be run when you have shown an overall statistically significant difference. Post hoc tests are termed a posteriori tests, that is, performed after the event (the event in this case being the overall analysis).

When Theory Isn't Uniform

A $\chi^2$ goodness-of-fit test assesses the total distance between the observed and theoretical distribution, a sum of the squared, relative differences!

Note that the theoretical proportions for each category do not have to be the same!

Ex: Are the six colors (green, orange, blue, red, yellow, brown) of M&Ms equally likely in a pack?

Ex: Does the grade distribution of the class follow the usual pattern?

What If Counts Are Very Few?

$\chi^2$ requires the following conditions:

  • When any elements are zero, $\chi^2$ is not possible (think about the process).
  • When any value is $\phantom{}< 10$, the Gaussian assumptions in $\chi^2$ fails.
  • $\chi^2$ is generally defined with free marginals.

For example, let's look at this small table:

Diabetes No Diabetes
Male 5 1
Female 1 4

If we demand that row and table totals (marginals) remain constant, then there are a finite number of possible tables. In this case, there are 6:

$${\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}6&0\\0&5\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}5&1\\1&4\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}3&3\\3&2\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}2&4\\4&1\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}1&5\\5&0\end{pmatrix}}.} $$

Thus, there are only 6 possible $\chi^2$ values: $0.11,0.78,2.4,4.41,7.64, 11.00$.

Which makes it hard to interpret the $\chi^2$ distribution!

Free Marginals in Chi-Squared Analysis

In chi-squared analysis, free marginals refer to contingency tables where row and column totals are not pre-determined but observed from the data. This setup is common in observational studies where the researcher does not control how data falls into categories.

The chi-squared test assesses whether there's an association between row and column variables, assuming that the observed frequencies in the table arise purely due to the underlying relationship between these variables.

Expected frequencies are calculated as:

$$E_{ij} = \frac{(Row\ Total_i \times Column\ Total_j)}{Grand\ Total}$$

Here, each expected count \(E_{ij}\) is based on the proportionate contribution of the corresponding row and column totals to the grand total, reflecting the independence assumption of the test.

Background of Fisher's Exact Test

Fisher's Exact Test was developed from an intriguing real-life scenario involving Muriel Bristol, a scientist at the Rothamsted Experiment Station. Bristol claimed she could discern whether milk or tea was poured first into a cup.

To test her assertion, Ronald Fisher devised a simple experiment: eight cups of tea were prepared, four with milk poured first and four with tea poured first. The marginals-four cups for each method-were fixed.

Bristol was then asked to taste each cup and identify the preparation method for four of them. This setup created an ideal scenario for using Fisher's Exact Test to determine if Bristol's claims were statistically significant, given the fixed margins and small sample size.

Stress the importance of the context in which Fisher's Exact Test was developed. Discuss its relevance to small sample sizes and fixed marginals. Mention that this anecdote illustrates how statistical methods can be applied to solve practical, everyday problems.

Fisher's Exact Test

By fixing the marginals, Fisher could then determine the probability of getting the exact table observed.

He did this by combinatorics:

B1 B2 Row Total
A1 $a$ $b$ $a + b$
A2 $c$ $d$ $c + d$
Column Total $a + c$ $b + d$ $n$

$${\displaystyle p_{\text{table}} = \frac{\dbinom{a + b}{a} \dbinom{c + d}{c}}{\dbinom{n}{a + c}} }$$

This gets pretty messy when expanded to factorial form:

$${\displaystyle \frac{ {(a+b)!(c+d)!(a+c)!(b+d)!} }{a!b!c!d!n!}}$$

$${\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&1\\2&2\\3&6\\4&24\\5&120\\6&720\\7&5,040\\8&40,320\\9&362,880\\10&3,628,800\\11&39,916,800\\12&479,001,600\\13&6,227,020,800\\14&87,178,291,200\end{pmatrix}}}$$

But this gets crazy as the numbers get large, which can be seen even by just looking at the table of the first 15 factorials:

  • (a+b/a): Ways to choose a successes in the first row
  • (c+d/c): Ways to choose c successes in the second row
  • (n/a+c): Total ways to assign a+c to the first column

Fisher's Exact Test: Tea Experiment

Bristol was presented with 8 cups of tea, 4 with milk poured first and 4 with tea poured first, and asked to identify the preparation method for each cup.

Milk 1st (Actual) Tea 1st (Actual)
Milk 1st (Guessed) 4 0
Tea 1st (Guessed) 0 4

${\displaystyle p=\frac{(4+0)!\,(0+4)!\,(4+0)!\,(0+4)!}{4!\,0!\,0!\,4!\,(4+0+0+4)!}}= 0.0142857$

And all the probabilities (sorted):

$\{ 0.0142857,\quad 0.0857143,\quad 0.2285714,\quad 0.2285714\}$

Since she correctly identified all 8 cups, she had a probability of $0.0142857$ of getting it right by chance.

Is this proof of her ability?

A p-value of 0.014 is strong evidence against the null hypothesis that she was guessing, but Fisher emphasized this didn't prove her ability but demonstrated the framework for statistical inference.

Again, no standard approach to the post-hoc, but it wasn't needed here.

Fisher's Exact Test: Diabetes

Looking back at the diabetes table, we can see that the probability of the observed table is $0.08$.

Diabetes No Diabetes
Male 5 1
Female 1 4

${\displaystyle p_{diabetes}=\frac{ (5+1)!\, (1+4)!\, (5+1)!\, (1+4)!\, }{ 5!\,1!\,1!\,4!\,(5+1+1+4)! }}= 0.0649351$

And all the probabilities (sorted):

$\{ 0.0021645,\quad 0.012987,\quad 0.0649351,\quad 0.0162338,\quad 0.324675,\quad 0.4329\}$

So we sum all probabilities less than or equal to the observed:
$p = \sum\{ 0.0021645,\quad 0.012987,\quad 0.0162338,\quad 0.0649351 \}=$ 0.096

With a p-value of 0.096, we fail to reject the null hypothesis at the conventional $\alpha=0.05$ level.

While there appears to be some association between sex and diabetes status in this sample, we don't have strong enough evidence to conclude this relationship exists in the population.

Again, no standard approach to the post-hoc, but it wasn't needed here.
