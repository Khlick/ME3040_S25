Proportional

(one variable)

  • Observe number of simple events over the course of an experiment.
  • Use a histogram, or frequency table, or dotplot, to "tally" or visualize the relative frequencies.
  • Construct a Null distribution and determine the relationship between the observed frequency and the expected frequency.

We ask whether the proportion of events we observe is related to, i.e. governed by, the probability of happening by random chance.

Inferential

(one or two variables)

  • Measure some quantitative variables and analyze the values.
  • We use a dot plot, or histogram, to visualize the shape of the distribution(s). For bivariate data, we can use a scatter plot to visualize the shape of the relationship.
  • Construct a confidence interval around statistics that describe the data, e.g. median, MAD, mode, \(\rho\).

We consider the possible range over which our chosen statistics are related to the population parameters.

Predictive

(two or more variables)

  • Measure some quantitative variables, construct a statistical model describing the observed relationship and then predict new outcomes based on new data.
  • We enhance visualizations with curves, lines and dimensions to show relationship details.
  • Construct a confidence interval around fit-parameters that describe the relationship.

We consider the possible range over which our model, i.e. the regression line, can explain the variance in our response variable using our measured predictors.

Proportional can be thought of as the coin flip example, or dice roll, where we used a geometric or binomial distribution to assess the empirical probability

Chi-Squared ($\chi^2$)

A statistic that is widely used to describe the difference in distribution patterns of categorical variable(s) is the square of the relative differences of each observed frequencies compared to the expected frequencies. This is known as the Chi-Squared statistic for comparing frequencies of a categorical variable.

$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{(\mathrm{Observed}_i-\mathrm{Expected}_i)^2}{\mathrm{Expected}_i}$

$\chi^2$ summarizes the discrepancy between observed and expected frequencies:

  • The smaller the overall discrepancy between the observed and expected scores, the smaller the value of the $\chi^2$.
  • Conversely, the larger the discrepancy between the observed and expected scores, the larger the value of the $\chi^2$.

Recall, chi-squared is the statistic we use to sum up the relative differences in COUNTS from corresponding bins of 2 distributions.

Differences Between Bins

Calculating a \( \chi^2 \) statistic is similar to determining normalized squared residuals in a linear regression model, applied to categorical data in histograms. It assesses deviations of observed categories from expected outcomes under a hypothesis.

How significantly does the distribution pattern of observed frequencies differ from the theoretical distribution? This analysis mirrors the evaluation of sum of squares between groups in ANOVA, which measures variance due to different groupings within the data.

Key idea

When we calculate a \( \chi^2 \) statistic to compare distribution patterns of categorical variables, this is called a \( \chi^2 \) goodness of fit test.

Note that here we compare our data to a theoretical distribution, when else did we do that?

How do we compare the observed pattern to a theoretical pattern?

How far are the observed counts from the expected (theoretical) counts if the null hypothesis ($\pi_{\text{rock}} = \pi_{\text{paper}} = \pi_{\text{scissors}}$) is true?

Expected Frequency

The expected counts are the amounts we should see in each category (cell) if the null hypothesis was true for that sample size.

$\mathrm{Expected}_i = n_\mathrm{total}\times\pi_i$

For each cell, the expected count is the total sample size ($n_\mathrm{total}$) times the null proportion for each hand sign, $\pi_\mathrm{i}$.

$\chi^2$ Goodness Of Fit

When we calculate a $\chi^2$ statistic to compare distribution patterns of categorical variables, this is called a $\chi^2$ goodness of fit test.

$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{(\mathrm{Observed}_i-\mathrm{Expected}_i)^2}{\mathrm{Expected}_i}$

Rock Paper Scissors
Observed 107 124 150
Expected 127 127 127

$\chi^2=\frac{(107-127)^2}{127}+\frac{(124-127)^2}{127}+\frac{(150-127)^2}{127}$

$\chi^2=3.150+0.071+4.165$

$\chi^2=7.386$

$\chi^2$ Distribution: Theory

If each of the expected counts are at least 10 (validity condition), AND if the null hypothesis is true, then the $\chi^2$ statistic follows a $\chi^2$-distribution, with degrees of freedom equal to:

$df = (\textrm{number of categories} - 1)$

Follow Up

We found a difference in distribution pattern that may be considered statistically significant.

So, what's next

The $\chi^2$ test tells you whether you have an overall difference between the observed and expected pattern, but it does not tell you which specific groups differ: post hoc tests do.

Note: Because post hoc tests are run to confirm where the differences occurred between groups, they should only be run when you have shown an overall statistically significant difference. Post hoc tests are termed a posteriori tests, that is, performed after the event (the event in this case being the overall analysis).

Follow Up

We found a difference in the distributional pattern considered statistically significant ($\text{p-value}\lt 0.05$)

So, what's next

No standard approach to follow, there are many different follow-up approaches possible.

We want to know if one of the hand sign (rock, paper, scissors) is used more often compared to the others so we could play the opposite sign more often to get an advantage.

Now that we know that there is a statistical difference in the distribution pattern of the signs, we could use pairwise analysis of difference in proportions to determine exactly which signs are more often played (i.e.: Tukey post hoc test).

Definitions: $\chi^2$

Chi-Squared Test

A statistical test used to determine if there is a significant difference between the expected frequencies and the observed frequencies in one or more categories.

Expected Frequency

The frequency expected in a category if the null hypothesis is true, calculated as the total sample size multiplied by the expected proportion under the null hypothesis.

Observed Frequency

The actual frequency observed in the sample for a category.

Chi-Squared Statistic

A measure of the discrepancy between the observed and expected frequencies, calculated using the formula: \( \chi^2 = \sum \frac{{(Observed - Expected)^2}}{Expected} \).

Degrees of Freedom

In the context of the chi-squared test, the number of degrees of freedom is the number of categories minus one.

Goodness of Fit

A type of chi-squared test that assesses how well the observed distribution of data matches with the expected distribution if no relationship exists in the population.
