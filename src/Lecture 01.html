Experimental Methods

The Engineering Method

In engineering, success or failure is not a destination, but a transient in the perpetuity of the scientific process. It is through iteration and recursion of scientific methods that innovation is born, solutions are refined, and progress is made. Each cycle is a step forward, turning uncertainty into knowledge, and transforming challenges into opportunities for greatness.

Underlying all of STEM, and particularly in the field of engineering, is the fundamental principle of the scientific method - a rigorous, systematic approach that is the bedrock of discovery and innovation. It's this methodical process of observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and analysis that empowers engineers to transform theoretical ideas into tangible realities. In this journey, statistics play a pivotal role, providing the tools to navigate through data, discern patterns, and extract meaningful insights. It's through statistical analysis that we quantify uncertainty, assess reliability, and validate our findings, turning raw data into valuable information that guides our decision-making and propels engineering advancements.

Example: The Cholera Epidemics

First truly global disease and also the most feared.
Symptoms of this disease include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain and a horribly violent case of diarrhea that can cause one to lose as much as 20 liters of fluid and electrolytes per day, leading to extremely severe dehydration.

Why Cholera in Engineering?

  • Improved Water and Sanitation Systems:
    • Modern sewer systems built to prevent contamination.
    • Separation of drinking water from sewage.
  • Public Health Advances:
    • Water filtration and treatment became standard.
    • Establishment of public health boards.
  • Urban Planning:
    • Introduction of zoning regulations for water and sewage systems.
  • Mapping Techniques:
    • Early use of mapping in public health led to modern GIS.

The first outbreak began in India early in the 19th century and spread to England in 1831.
Cholera's mortality rate was about 20 percent, and the nature of how it spread was the subject of intense professional debate.

Finally, in London, is where Cholera's reign of terror was finally overthrown.

Cholera Theories: Is it in the air?

  • Miasma Theory (airborne transmission)
  • Prevailing dogma of the 19th century was that all disease and epidemics stemmed from "Bad Air".
  • "Bad Air", called miasmata, is a poisonous vapor with suspended particles of decaying matter and a foul smell.
  • John Snow: A British physician. Skeptical of the Miasma Theory.
  • He proposed that cholera was attributable to a "self-replicating agent", probably in the drinking water.
  • This is a Scientific Hypothesis!

The prevailing theory was that it was airborne, and they believed this of most disease at the time.

Worse, because cholera afflicted people in poor areas, who smelled badly, they thought it was a poor-person's disease.

A newly minted physician, John Snow, who mainly focused on the beginnings of anesthesia for obstetrics, thought cholera might be from contaminated water.

In 1854, a suburb of London call Soho was hit hard with a cholera outbreak. Snow lived nearby and got to work investigating.

He had a hypothesis!

Types of Hypotheses

Generally, a hypothesis is an assumption about certain characteristics of an object of interest/study. This assumption may or may not be true.

A scientific hypothesis is a specific statement proposed as an explanation for a phenomenon, regardless of truth.

A statistical hypothesis is a specific statement about a characteristic or parameter of the data.

Scientific

  • Is farming salmon cost prohibitive?
  • Does smoking cause lung cancer?
  • Does thermal cycling cause premature failure in turbine blades?

Statistical

  • Is the average length of farmed salmon greater than wild salmon?
  • Are Diacetyl concentrations and lung cancer cell-division rate correlated?
  • Is the mean time-to-failure of thermally cycled turbine blades less than non-cycled blades?
  • General: An assumption that may or may not be true.
  • Scientific: A specific proposal as an explanation for some phenomenon.
  • Statistical: A specific statement about some aspect of the DATA.

These are clear distinctions. We need to be careful that we don't confuse them.

Data Collection

The population is the entire collection of observational units we are interested in. A sample is a subset of the population on which we record data.

Numerical summaries about a population are called parameters, and numerical summaries calculated from a sample are called statistics.

Once we have our requirements, and we have an idea about what to measure, we go out into the world, and we get data.

Now, we can't collect all the data, so we collect a sample of the data.

If this population was Normal distributed, with parameters mu and sigma, what test would we use to ask if our sample might have come from that population? Z-test

The size of the sample is important.
The bigger the sample size, the more likely the data we collect can be generalized to the full population.

Are there are problems with using a general total of houses supplied by each company as a denominator?

  • Ideally, the denominator should be all people supplied by water from each company, not houses. Different houses may contain different numbers of people. If, for example, the Lambeth company supplied an area where there were consistently more people per house than the area supplied by the Southwark and Vauxhall company, then we might be misled by using the number of houses as a denominator.
  • Ideally, the denominator should be houses in the areas of mixed supply only, not all households supplied by the companies, because the patrons could differ substantially over the larger area.

Why Cholera in Engineering?

  1. Improvement in Water Supply and Sanitation Systems:
    • Development of Modern Sewer Systems: Overhaul of sanitation infrastructure, leading to extensive sewer systems to reduce waterborne diseases.
    • Separation of Drinking Water and Sewage: Separation of sewage from drinking water supplies to reduce cholera and other waterborne diseases.
  2. Advances in Public Health Policies:
    • Introduction of Water Filtration and Treatment: Filtration and chlorination became standard to ensure safe drinking water.
    • Establishment of Public Health Boards: Creation of public health institutions to monitor and manage health risks, with a focus on preventive measures.
  3. Urban Planning and Zoning:
    • Zoning Regulations for Water and Sewage Systems: Regulations introduced to protect water sources from contamination, influencing urban planning.
  4. Mapping and Geographic Information Systems (GIS):
    • Use of Mapping in Public Health: Snow's use of maps for cholera cases laid the groundwork for modern GIS, essential in public health and urban planning.
  5. Influence on the Germ Theory of Disease:
    • Snow's work supported the shift away from miasma theory to germ theory, advancing public health practices.

Statistical Investigation

The Why And The How

Statistical Purpose

To answer a question about a population (of data).

In a perfect world, we would have access to the full population so we would be able to answer the question based on data collected on all the observational units of the population. We would know the value of the true parameter we are interested in.

But usually we just have one sample.

Statistics is about using a sample, or many samples,

to make inferences about the population parameters.

Statistical Inference

There are 4 main types of inferences that we can draw from data:

  • Significance
  • Estimation
  • Generalization
  • Causation

Statistics is all about gaining knowledge (drawing conclusions) about a population data by only using a piece of this population (sample).

This process is called drawing inferences.
