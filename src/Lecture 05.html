Central Limit Theorem

From the central limit theorem, we assume that the sampling distribution of any common statistic should be normally distributed, if a large number of independent, identically distributed samples are taken.

Consider a single sample (Size N) and the statistic: Mean.

What about other statistics?

In the 18th century, a few mathematicians, abraham de moivre, and later Pierre-Simon Laplace then Carl Gauss and others, observed that if you looked at the averages of many samples, the distribution of those averages converged on the normal distribution. Even when the samples were taken from a underlying distribution which was not normally distributed.

Adjust parameters, run simulations.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the distribution of the sum (or average) of a large number of independent, identically distributed random variables, each with a finite mean and variance, will approximate a normal distribution, regardless of the original distribution of the variables. This convergence towards normality occurs as the sample size increases, underscoring the normal distribution's significance in probability and statistics.

The Normal Distribution

The normal (or Gaussian) distribution is a continuous probability distribution characterized by a symmetric, bell-shaped curve.

PDF:

$$f(\class{warning}{x}; \class{info}{\mu}, \class{info}{\sigma}) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$$
  • $\mu$ = Central Tendency ($E[X]$)
  • 68% of the observations lie within 1 standard "distance" of the center
  • $\sigma$ = Spread ($E[X-E[X]]$)
  • 95% lie within 1.96 standard "distance" of the center
  • $x$ = Specific value of the continuous variable
  • 99% lie within 2.58 standard "distance" of the center

The Empirical Rule (or 68-95-99.7 rule) is a simplification that describes how much of the data falls within 1, 2, or 3 standardized distances from the center (Expected Value) of the distribution.

While the Empirical Rule gives us a rough sense of the spread of data in a normal distribution, how can we assess whether real-world data actually follows this pattern?

Example: Normal Distribution

Example: In auditory biomechanics research, the Young's modulus of the human tympanic membrane is assumed to be normally distributed with a mean ($\mu$) of 45 MPa and a standard deviation ($\sigma$) of 10 MPa.

What is the probability that a randomly sampled eardrum tissue has a Young's modulus at least as great as 55 MPa?

$P(X \geq 55) = 1 - P(X < 55) =$ $$1 - \int_{-\infty}^{55} f(x)\,dx$$

Where $f(x)\,dx$ is the PDF ($f(x;\mu,\, \sigma)$), then $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1$ is the cumulative density function.

Cumulative Density Function

The Emprical Rule is based on fixed interval ($\pm1\sigma,\, \pm2\sigma,\, \pm3\sigma,\, \ldots$), but a more general approach to understanding the distributional properties of a variable is to use the Cumulative Density Function.

The Cumulative Density Function (CDF) expresses the probability that a random variable is less than or equal to a given value:

$$F(x)=P(X\leq x)$$

The CDF of a Normal distirbution gives a smooth S-shaped curve thatstarts at 0 and increases to 1 as you move along the x-axis, with the rate of increase depending on the distribution of the variable. Thus, you can directly read probabilities for any threshold (not just $\pm1\sigma,\, \pm2\sigma,\, \pm3\sigma,\, \ldots$)

To calculate the probability of a value being more than some threshold, we calculate the lower tail of the distribution and subtract from 1.

Recall 68% of values lie within 1 standardized distance, so 32% are in symmetrical "extremes", thus $\approx 16\%$ in the upper extreme, for this example (45 MPa, 10 MPa), this is $P(X\geq55)=1-P(X\leq55)=1-0.8413=0.1587$

The Normal Distribution

The Normal distribution's shape indicates values closer to the center are more probable.

Standard Normal Distribution:

The standard normal distribution is a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1, denoted as $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. It serves as a reference for converting any normal distribution to this standard form using the Z-score.

Z-Score:

The Z-score represents the number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean. It is calculated using the formula:

$$Z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$$

where $x$ is the data point, $\mu$ the mean, and $\sigma$ the standard deviation.

To determine if our data are normal, we need to understand more about how the data are distributed...

PDF:
$f(\class{warning}{x}; \class{info}{\mu}, \class{info}{\sigma}) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$

CDF:
$P(\class{smaller}{-\infty \leq \class{warning}{x} \leq \infty})\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(\class{warning}{x}; \class{info}{\mu}, \class{info}{\sigma})\,dx = 1$

From the tympanic membrane Young's modulus example, what would the Z-score of the 55MPa value be? Z=1

The theoretical normal CDF gives us an expected cumulative probability, but how do we compute this from observed data?

Well, we could ASSUME a normal distribution, then simply use the FORMULA for the CDF. But with real world data, we don't really know if it is normal.

We can use the empirical cumulative density function (ECDF) to estimate the CDF from the data.

The Empirical CDF

Empirical Cumulative Density Function (ECDF)

A step function that estimates the cumulative distribution function of a sample by mapping each data point to its corresponding percentile rank within the sample

\(\hat{F}(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} I(X_i \le x)\)

Sample-based estimate of the CDF!

The Empirical Rule gives interval-based probabilities assuming normality.

The Empirical CDF gives direct empirical probabilities from observed data without assumptions of any kind.

Understanding Quantiles

Quantiles are values that mark where specific proportions of your data fall below.

The quantile function is generally defined as: $\class{success}{Q_{i}(p) = (1 - \gamma)x_{j} + \gamma x_{j+1}}$,
where $j = \lfloor k \rfloor$ for $k = np + m$ and $\gamma = k - j$.

For quantile method 8 (unbiased median), the quantile function is defined as
\(Q_{8}(p) = (1 - \gamma)x_{j} + \gamma x_{j+1}\),
where \(j=\lfloor k \rfloor\), \(m = \frac{p + 1}{3}\), \(k = np + m\), and \(\gamma = k - j\).

Reference: Hyndman and Fan (1996) for a detailed explanation on quantile types and their applications.

\(Q_{8}(\class{danger}{0.5}) = (1 - \class{pop}{\gamma}) x_{\class{info}{5}} + \class{pop}{\gamma}x_{\class{info}{5}+1} = (1-\class{pop}{0.5}) 0.411 + (\class{pop}{0.5})0.95 = 0.6805\)

Calculating the median from taking mean of middle 2 elements:
$ m = \frac{0.411 + 0.95}{2} = 0.6805$

What is the data space (units) of the quantile function? The same as the data. That is, we get a location in the dataset where the pth fraction of the number of data points falls below.

How does this relate to the normal distribution? We can use a similar approach and map our data points to the cdf values of the normal distribution. To do that, we need another function.

Quantile Probability Points

Probability points are used to determine the specific quantiles of a distribution for a given dataset of size N. These points help in mapping data to their respective quantiles in a theoretical distribution.

For a dataset of size \(N\), the \(i^{th}\) probability point \(p_i\) is calculated as:

\(p_i = \frac{i - \alpha}{N + 1 - 2\alpha}\),
where \(i\) ranges from 1 to \(N\), and \(\alpha\) is an adjustment factor (commonly 0.5 or \(\frac{3}{8}\) for small samples).

Example for \(N = 10\):

  • Using \(\alpha = 0.5\), calculate \(p_i\) for \(i = 1\) to \(10\).
  • \(p_1 = \frac{1 - 0.5}{10 + 1 - 2 \times 0.5} = \frac{0.5}{10} = 0.05\)
  • Similarly, \(p_{10} = \frac{10 - 0.5}{10 + 1 - 2 \times 0.5} = \frac{9.5}{10} = 0.95\)
  • \(N < 10\): \(\alpha=0.375\)
  • \(10 \leq N < 50\): \(\alpha=0.4\)
  • \(N \geq 50\): \(\alpha = 0.5\)

Now we can map our sample size to the cumulative probability points in the normal distribution. More specifically, to the standard normal with mean 0 and standard deviation of 1.

But, recall I said the standard normal distribution assumes values on the interval -infty to + infty? We have to deal with that.

Probability Points And The ECDF

Empirical Cumulative Density Function (ECDF)

A step function that estimates the cumulative distribution function of a sample by mapping each data point to its corresponding percentile rank within the sample

\(\hat{F}(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} I(X_i \le x)\)

Sample-based estimate of the CDF!

The formula for probability points is adjusted from the ECDF's simple \(1/n\) weighting to accommodate the range of the inverse quantile function of a normal distribution.

Specifically, as the inverse quantile function approaches infinity at \(p = 1\), using a midpoint approach \(\left(\frac{i - 0.5}{n}\right)\) prevents the calculation of impossible values and better aligns with the theoretical distribution, providing a more accurate comparison.

Recall the cumulative density function for the normal distribution is the sum of the probability density function. The total area under the curve is 1, the upper limit of the CDF.

Because the CDF (theory) expects values from -infty to +infty, we can't simply map our sample to the CDF. Instead, we use the empirical cumulative density function (ECDF) to estimate the CDF.

If we have probability points that map directly to the sample, we would end up with a cumulative density of 1, which would correspond to the +infinity value, so our probability points formula allows us to adjust for this, making the estimates from the standard normal distribution more accurate.

Choosing the right \(\alpha\) is crucial for accurate Q-Q plots:

  • Small Sample Sizes (\(N < 10\)): Use \(\alpha=0.375\) as suggested by Blom to minimize bias in the tails.
  • Moderate Sample Sizes (\(10 \leq N < 50\)): Use \(\alpha=0.4\) to improve the fit by balancing the mid-range and tail representations.
  • Large Sample Sizes (\(N \geq 50\)): Use \(\alpha = 0.5\) (median rank), which becomes increasingly reliable as the sample size grows, with minimal effect on the plot's accuracy.

Viewing Normality: QQ plot

  • The sample data are plotted against the standard normal (theoretical) quantiles.
  • The unity line represents perfect normality; sample points should cluster around this line.
  • Points on the unity line suggest that the sample distribution is similar to a normal distribution.
  • Deviation from the unity line implies potential skewness or kurtosis in the sample distribution.
  • The Quantile-Quantile Plot provides a visual comparison between empirical data quantiles and theoretical normal quantiles.

The QQ plot visualizes the sample quantiles against the theoretical quantiles from a standard normal distribution. A linear relationship suggests normality; deviations from this can inform about the distribution's skewness or tail behavior.

What about the uncertainty in the quantiles?

Quantile⇒Normal

The QQ plot compares sample quantiles to theoretical quantiles, with the grey shaded area representing a 95% confidence band obtained through simulation.

Points falling within this band are consistent with normal sampling variation, while points outside suggest significant departures from normality.

This uncertainty visualization helps distinguish between random sampling fluctuations and true non-normal features in the data's distribution.

While an important part of assessing normality, you should always confirm with other methods

In this case, the theoretical quantiles are the normal quantiles, but in general, they could be any distribution.

Interpreting QQ Plots

Deviations from the unity line have different implications:

  • Normal: Points closely follow the unity line, indicating a normal distribution.
  • Left Skew: Points curve below at the lower end and below the line at the upper end, reflecting a longer tail on the left and shorter on the right.
  • Right Skew: Points curve above the line at the lower end and above at the upper end, indicating a longer tail on the right and shorter on the left.

Interpreting QQ Plots

Deviations from the unity line have different implications:

  • Bimodal: Shows a distinctive "S" shaped curve or multiple clusters along the QQ line, indicating multiple peaks in the data distribution.
  • Fat Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, suggesting excess kurtosis and a higher likelihood of extreme values (i.e., longer tails at both ends).
  • Skinny Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, indicating a lower likelihood of extreme values (i.e., shorter tails on both sides).

Note that bimodal crosses in the middle and then also has skinny tails here. Where the skinny tails distribution doesn't cross so much (but might have multiple peaks randomly due to construction)

Beyond QQ plots, several statistical tests can assess normality:

  • Shapiro-Wilk Test
  • Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test
  • Anderson-Darling Test
  • Lilliefors Test

The Shapiro-Wilk test is particularly highlighted for its sensitivity in detecting deviations from normality.
