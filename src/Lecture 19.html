ANOVA Does Even More

One-Way ANOVA

One-Way ANOVA considers multiple groups belonging to a single categorical family called a factor level, i.e. sandwiches, treatment types, etc.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{SS_{between}}}{\color{lightblue}{SS_{Within}}}$$

One-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA

Just as we discussed with before $\sim$ after in two-group NHST, ANOVA has special methods for dealing with repeatedly measuring from a single subject. It takes the form of:

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{SS_{between}}}{\color{lightblue}{(SS_{Within}- SS_{random})}}=\frac{SS_{between.timepoints}}{SS_{fixed}}$$

N-Way ANOVA

We aren't limited to one level (plus time) and can model multiple factors, though it starts getting complicated because we have new kinds of terms to discuss called interactions. Note that each new factor level will get its own $\mathcal{F}$-statistic.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\{ SS_{b.group1},\, SS_{b.group2},\, \ldots ,\, SS_{b.groupN},\, \{ SS_{\color{orange}{interactions}}\} \} } {SS_{Within}}$$

N-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA

Just like in the One-Way approach, we now have to consider the effects of time and remove the random effects, i.e. the within subject variation.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\{ SS_{b.time},\, SS_{b.group2},\, \ldots ,\, SS_{b.groupN},\, \{ SS_{\color{orange}{interactions}}\} \} } {SS_{Within}-SS_{random}}$$

Note: As we add more and more factors, we stop calling it ANOVA and just say "Factor Analysis", and it starts taking on a different purpose.

Namely, Factor Analysis is a way of reducing data dimensionality by merging variables with highly explained variance.

The point here, is we use the F-Statistic to compare the residual error of our model to the variation between our groups. Where else do we see this? Linear Regression!

F-Statistic in Linear Regression

The F-statistic is used to determine the overall significance of the regression model, testing the null hypothesis that all regression coefficients are equal to zero versus at least one differs.

For linear regression, the F-statistic is calculated as:

$$ F = \frac{MSR}{MSE} $$

Where:

  • \(MSR\) (Mean Square Regression) = \(\frac{SSR}{DFR}\) and \(DFR = 1\) for simple linear regression
  • \(MSE\) (Mean Square Error) = \(\frac{SSE}{DFE}\) with \(DFE = n - p - 1\)
  • \(SSR\) (Sum of Squares for Regression) = \(SST - SSE\)
  • \(SST\) (Total Sum of Squares) = \(\sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2\)
  • \(SSE\) (Error Sum of Squares) = \(\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\)

The F-statistic allows us to assess whether our model provides a better fit to the data than a model with no predictors (only an intercept).

The F-statistic in simple linear regression helps to test the overall significance of the model. It's a way to see if our predictive model is better than an average value model.

F-Statistic in Linear Regression

Let's calculate the F-statistic for a simple linear regression model using a hypothetical dataset related material testing.

  • \(SST = 60\) (total sum of squares)
  • \(SSE = 45\) (residual sum of squares)
  • \(n = 25\) (sample size)
  • \(p = 1\) (number of predictors)

First, calculate \(SSR\) and then \(MSE\) and \(MSR\):

\(SSR = SST - SSE = 60 - 45 = 15\)

\(MSE = \frac{SSE}{n - p - 1} = \frac{45}{23} \approx 1.96\)

\(MSR = \frac{SSR}{DFR} = \frac{15}{1} = 15\)

Finally, the F-statistic is calculated as:

\(F = \frac{MSR}{MSE} = \frac{15}{1.96} \approx 7.65\)

Since \(F = 7.65\) is slightly larger than the critical value of \(F(1, 23) \approx 4.28\), we reject the null hypothesis, but the model's significance is only moderate.

In this example, the F-statistic of 7.65 is moderately above the critical value, suggesting that the predictor is likely significant but with limited strength. This can happen in mechanical engineering when testing material properties where a weak relationship exists.

The F-statistic is used to assess the overall goodness of fit in a regression model, particularly when comparing how well the model with predictors fits the data compared to a model with only an intercept (i.e., no predictors). Use the F-statistic when:

  • You want to test the overall significance of your regression model.
  • You're comparing a full model (with predictors) to a reduced model (no predictors or only an intercept).
  • Evaluating if the inclusion of predictors improves the model's fit.

When interpreting the F-statistic:

  • A high F-statistic suggests that the model fits significantly better than a model with no predictors.
  • A borderline F-statistic (like this example) might indicate some model utility, but additional evidence or a larger dataset might be needed to confirm the relationship.
  • A low F-statistic means that the predictors do not significantly improve the fit of the model, leading to retention of the null hypothesis.

Comparing Multiple Means

We have 3 groups. Why not just carry out a bunch of pair-wise analyses of the difference in mean?

$H_0:\, \mu_\class{warning}{1}=\mu_\class{info}{2}$

$H_0:\, \mu_\class{warning}{1}=\mu_\class{danger}{3}$

$H_0:\, \mu_\class{info}{2}=\mu_\class{danger}{3}$

Now, the question is, which filling or fillings are the ants in love with?

We have to consider what the false positive rate means.

Controlling For Type 1 Errors

Each test is carried out at $\alpha=0.05$, so the risk of making a Type I error (rejecting the null when we should not) is $5\%$ each time.

The risk of making a Type I errors increases as we multiply the number of tests on the same data:

  • At the $5\%$ significance level, the probability of making at least one type I error for 3 tests would be $14\%$
  • Comparing 4 means (6 tests), this jumps to $26\%$
  • Comparing 5 means (10 tests), this jumps to $40\%$

We need to make multiple comparisons with an overall Type I error of $\alpha=0.05$ (or whichever level is specified). We need an alternative approach that uses one overall test that compares all means at once.

Type 1 Errors Accumulate Rapidly

In a test at $\alpha = 0.05$, there is a 5% chance of getting a false positive if the null hypothesis is true. In 100 tests, each at $\alpha = 0.05$, we "expect 5 false positives" if all 100 null hypotheses are true, but really, the probability of getting $k$ false-positives is determinable through the binomial distribution, $p(k\, |\, \alpha)$.

We can calculate the probability of getting exactly one false positive, but we rarely care if there's only 1.

Instead, we care usually if one or more false positives occur.

This is the probability of getting a false positive in a single test.

The Multiple Testing Problem

The chance of a Type I error is $\alpha$

$\alpha$ of all tests with true null hypotheses will yield significant results just by chance

ex: If 100 tests are done with $\alpha=0.05$ and nothing is really going on (the null hypothesis is really true), 5% of them will yield significant results (reject the null), just by chance.

This is known as the problem of multiple testing

Consider a topic that is being investigated by research teams all over the world:
Using $\alpha=0.05$, 5% of teams are going to find something significant, even if the null hypothesis is true.

Consider a research team or company doing many hypothesis tests:
Using $\alpha=0.05$, 5% of the tests are going to be significant, even if the null hypotheses are all true. (multiple hypothesis tests)

When multiple tests are conducted, if the null hypotheses are all true, the proportion of all the tests that will yield statistically significant results just by random chance is about $\alpha$, the significance level.

How Do We Know About Errors?

Let's look at a study in Drosophila melanogaster (common fruit flies).

Scientist sought to understand if having the choice of a mate would have an effect on "fitness" of offspring.

Paper published in Nature with $\text{p-value}< 0.01$.

Concluded, based on the data, that mate choice improves offspring fitness (This went against conventional wisdom).

600 female fruit flies, randomly divided:

After mating, females were separated from the males and put in egg-laying chambers.

200 larvae from each chamber were taken and placed in a cage with 200 mutant flies (for competition). Offspring survival rate was measured.

How Do We Know About Errors?

In mate choice and offspring "fitness", we saw 2 studies with different outcomes:

One study found significant results,

and they reported $p < 0.01$.

One study found insignificant results,

and they reported $p=0.102$.

Conflicting results?

How can this be? It turns out that while we focus a lot on the p-value (and the alpha, or type 1, error rate), there's more to the "error-story".

Types Of Significance Testing Errors

Errors can, and do, happen!

  • A Type I error is rejecting a true null (false positive)
  • A Type II error is not rejecting a false null (false negative)

Testing is complicated, and time consuming, and you already double checked your design calculations, so you take a shortcut.
