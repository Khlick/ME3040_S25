One-Tailed Test

Used when we're interested if the test statistic ($\delta$) is significantly greater than or less than the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$).

It specifies the direction of the effect:

  • Right-tailed test: $\delta \gg H_0$.
  • Left-tailed test: $\delta \ll H_0$.

Two-Tailed Test

Used when we're interested in whether the test statistic ($\delta$) is different from $H_0$, without specifying the direction of the difference.

Us it to test for any significant difference.

Statistic Of Interest

In statistics and mathematics, it is often useful to define functions that operate on two variables in various ways. A general approach is to express a function $f$ that takes two inputs, $X$ and $Y$, and applies a specified operator to these inputs. This operator, denoted by $\circ$, fundamentally changes the way $X$ and $Y$ are processed within the function.

That is, generally we can say:
$\delta\left( X,Y\right) = f(X,Y;\circ)$, where $X\, \&\, Y$ are independent random variables and $f\left(X,Y;\circ\right)$ means: "apply the function $f$ to $X\,\&\,Y$ using $\circ$ as the operator."

Example: If $f$ is the mean and $\circ$ is $-$, then
$f(X,Y;-) = \overline{Y} - \overline{X}$.

Comparison Type Notation Interpretation
Difference $ f(X, Y; -) = f(Y) - f(X) $ Compares $f(Y)$ and $f(X)$ by subtraction.
Ratio $ f(X, Y; /) = \frac{f(Y)}{f(X)} $ Compares $f(Y)$ and $f(X)$ as a ratio.
Sum $ f(X, Y; +) = f(Y) + f(X) $ Adds $f(Y)$ and $f(X)$.
Product $ f(X, Y; \cdot) = f(Y) \cdot f(X) $ Multiplies $f(Y)$ and $f(X)$.
Correlation $ f(X, Y; \text{corr}) = \rho_{f(X)f(Y)} $ Measures the linear relationship between $f(X)$ and $f(Y)$.
Regression $ f(X, Y; \text{reg}) = \beta_1 $ Represents the slope of $f(Y)$ on $f(X)$ in a linear regression model.

We can call the application of f on X and Y the statistic, such that if f is the mean, then f(X,Y;circ) = barY - barX!

William Sealy Gosset

An influential statistician, William Sealy Gosset worked at the Guinness brewery in Dublin, Ireland. He developed the t-distribution under the pseudonym "Student."

Gosset's work was pivotal for small sample statistics, leading to the development of the Student's t-test, a fundamental tool in statistical hypothesis testing.

William Gosset's contribution to statistics while working in a practical field highlights the importance of real-world data analysis.

T-Distribution

The t-distribution is a probability distribution that arises when estimating the mean of a normally distributed population with a small sample size, and the population standard deviation is unknown. It is similar to the normal distribution but has heavier tails, reflecting greater variability.

The t-statistic is defined as:

$$ t_{\widehat{\beta}}=\frac{\widehat{\beta}-\beta_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}(\widehat{\beta})} $$

where the t-distribution pdf is defined as:

$$ t_{\mathrm{pdf}}=\textstyle\frac{\Gamma \left(\frac{\nu+1}{2} \right)} {\sqrt{\nu\pi}\,\Gamma \left(\frac{\nu}{2} \right)} \left(1+\frac{x^2}{\nu} \right)^{-\frac{\nu+1}{2}}\! $$

for $\nu$ degrees of freedom greater than 0 and $\in\mathbb{R}$.

T-Distribution

The t-distribution is a probability distribution that arises when estimating the mean of a normally distributed population with a small sample size, and the population standard deviation is unknown. It is similar to the normal distribution but has heavier tails, reflecting greater variability.

The t-statistic is defined as:

$$ t_{\widehat{\beta}}=\frac{\widehat{\beta}-\beta_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}(\widehat{\beta})} $$

where the t-distribution pdf is defined as:

$$ t_{\mathrm{pdf}}=\textstyle\frac{\Gamma \left(\frac{\nu+1}{2} \right)} {\sqrt{\nu\pi}\,\Gamma \left(\frac{\nu}{2} \right)} \left(1+\frac{x^2}{\nu} \right)^{-\frac{\nu+1}{2}}\! $$

for $\nu$ degrees of freedom greater than 0 and $\in\mathbb{R}$.

Key Properties:

  • Shape: Symmetrical and bell-shaped, like the normal distribution.
  • Variability: More spread out with heavier tails, indicating a higher probability of extreme values.
  • Degrees of Freedom (df): Determines the exact shape; calculated as the sample size minus one ($n - 1$). As the degrees of freedom increase, the t-distribution approaches the standard normal distribution.

Usage:

  • Estimating population means when the sample size is small (typically $n < 30$).
  • The population standard deviation is unknown.
  • The underlying population is assumed to be normally distributed.
  • May be used for small sample size Confidence Intervals.

Student's t-test

The Student's t-test, created by Gosset, is used to determine if there is a significant difference between the means of two groups, which may be related in certain features.

Let's assume the unknown variance in our samples is "equal".

$$t=\frac{(\overline{X_1}-\overline{X_2})\class{danger}{-(\mu_1-\mu_2)}}{\sqrt{S_p^2\left( \frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}$$

We compare our $t$ to the T-distribution, defined with a Degrees Of Freedom parameter: $\nu=n_1+n_2-2$.

Because $S_p$ is the pooled estimate of the "equal variance", you'll see it called the "pooled t-test".

$$S_p^2 = \frac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}$$

For the case of testing, "are two different samples different from each other?" We can define some parameters.

Pause on the formula, ask about the pooled variance divided by n. Contemplate, the standard error

The red text represents what we call the null parameter, more on the next slide.

The formula represents the t-value calculation, a crucial component in hypothesis testing.

What does this formula state? There are two parts:

  1. The difference in means between two variables (Xs) varies from the difference in means between the underlying population distribution means (mus) on the t-scale
  2. The sampling distribution of the t-scale has a spread of the squared root of 2 times the shared variance and is dependent on the sample size (nu)

Theory: Two-sample Student's $t$-test

Comparing two groups:

  • H$_0$ : $\bar{x}_1 = \bar{x}_2$ (Null parameter: $\mu_1=\mu_2 = 0$)
  • SOI: $\class{small}{mean\left(\bar{x}_1,\bar{x}_2;-\right) = \bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}$

Key idea: The strategy to assess significance with a theoretical approach is to study the sampling distribution of the Statistic Of Interest.

Once again, this relies heavily on the Central Limit Theorem.
The statistic we will obtain (the t statistic) will characterize how far away from the center of the sampling distribution is our initial observed statistic in term of standard deviation (SE, since it's a sampling distribution).

Theory: Two-sample Student's $t$-test

If two samples are coming from the same underlying normal population, we can estimate the standard deviation of this pooled population with $s^2_\mathrm{pooled} = \frac{s_1^2(n_1-1)+s_2^2(n_2-1)}{n_1+n_2-2}$ and the standard error (standard deviation of the sampling distribution) of the difference between means of two samples from this distribution with $SE = \sqrt{\frac{s_\mathrm{pooled}}{n_1}+\frac{s_\mathrm{pooled}}{n_2}}$, where $s_1$ and $s_2$ are the standard deviations in the two samples and $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the sizes of the two samples.

Note: When we use the samples standard deviations in estimating SE (we don't know the standard deviation $s$ of the underlying populations), we need to switch to a t-distribution rather than the standard normal when computing a p-value.

Two-sample Student's $t$-test

For a two-sample $t$-test, you are making the (strong) assumption that your samples are coming from a pooled population normally distributed. You can then compute the ($t$) distribution of the difference in means from 2 samples from this underlying population.

Two-sample Student's $t$-test

$t=\frac{\textrm{Statistic}-\textrm{Null value}}{SE}$

$t=\frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0}{\sqrt{\frac{S_\mathrm{pooled}}{n_1} + \frac{S_\mathrm{pooled}}{n_2}}}$

with $S_\mathrm{pooled} = \frac{s_1^2(n_1-1)+s_2^2(n_2-1)}{n_1+n_2-2}$

$S_\mathrm{pooled}=\frac{6.25^2\times(26-1)+4.89^2\times(30-1)}{26+30-2}=30.9$

$t=\frac{(108.34-107.81)-0}{\sqrt{\frac{30.9}{26}+\frac{30.9}{30}}}=0.36$

The initial observation is $0.36$ SE away from the center of the (null) distribution.
Using this $t$-statistic & the degrees of freedom of the $t$-distribution, we can obtain a p-value by referring to a $t$-statistic table.

Two Sample NHST (t-test)

We calculate the p-value: $p=0.7203$ Fail To Reject $H_0$!

Does this mean we have "proven" the alternate hypothesis?

Interpreting the p-value

Assuming equal mean commute times in the long term, the observed difference$\phantom{}^1$ of 0.53 minutes would occur roughly 72% of the time with random assignments.

No significant evidence exists to suggest a long-term difference in commute times between the two bikes.

Does this prove the choice of bike doesn't affect commute time?

No. A high p-value does not confirm the null hypothesis; it only suggests it's a plausible explanation.

A small but real long-term difference may still exist.

What is the Null Distribution

8 fragments. Imagine you have taken the two samples (red and blue).

Fragment 1: The null hypothesis says, these two samples are part of the same distribution so we can put them into one big group, that is, breaking their relationships to their respective groups,e.g. frame material in this case.

Fragment 2: we can then pretend we are taking 2 more random selections from this big group, and then computing these new hypothetical group means and calculating the difference in their means.

The null distribution then, is the sampling distribution of the the mean difference. Like we took many (run fragments here) many random samples.

This sampling distribution of the mean differences of the decoupled groups is the null distribution, which will be centered at H0.
