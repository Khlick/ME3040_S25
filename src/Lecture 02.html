Frequentism and Bayesian Probability

The Frequentist's Approach

The frequentist's perspective emphasizes the importance of repeated trials in understanding probability.

  • Views probability as long-term event frequency.
  • Based on repeated, identical trials.

The Bayesian Approach

Bayesian probability focuses on subjective belief rather than objective data.

  • Defines probability as a measure of belief, 0 to 1 scale.
  • Subjective, based on opinion rather than inherent system traits.

2 major approaches, while they treat probabilities similarly, they differ in their interpretations.

Real-world: breast cancer. Prevalence (13%, death 2.6%) of women. Mammograms are good, but not great, meaning it can miss some cancers, and misdiagnose non-cancers, all of which lead to poor prognosis for the patient. So how do we decide how often and who should get tested?

Freq. probabilities are interpreted as the long-run frequency of events. They analyze the probability of a positive test result in terms of the relative frequencies observed in large numbers of similar patients. They use sensitivity (true pos) and specificity (true neg) of the test, along with prev. of that cancer to calculate the probability of having cancer given a + test result.

Bayes: Probabilities are degrees of belief, subjective probability, base on prior knowledge. Bayesian would start with a prior probability of having breast cancer (based on factors like age, family history, and population prevalence). Upon receiving a test result (positive or negative), the Bayesian updates this probability, also incorporating the sensitivity and specificity of the test. Bayesian approach is to update prior belief (probability) with additional data, in this case to produce the posterior probability of having cancer given the test.

What is Probability?

Probability is the measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. It quantifies uncertainty and is expressed as a number between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates impossibility and 1 indicates certainty.

Terminology

Meaning

Sample Space ($S$):

The set of all possible outcomes of a probabilistic experiment. For example, in a quality test of a car engine, the sample space might include outcomes like 'Pass' and 'Fail'.

Event:

A subset of the sample space. An event can consist of one or more outcomes. For instance, the event 'Engine Fails' includes all outcomes where the engine fails the quality test.

Probability of an Event ($P(A)$):

The likelihood of an event occurring, which is a number between 0 and 1. If $P(A) = 1$, the event is certain to happen, and if $P(A) = 0$, the event is impossible. For example, if the probability of an engine passing the quality test is 0.95, it can be written as $P(\text{Engine Passes}) = 0.95$.

Approaches

Probability theory is a fundamental part of mathematics and statistics that deals with the likelihood of different outcomes in various scenarios. There are three primary approaches to understanding and calculating probability:

Approach Description
Subjective Based on personal judgment and available information. It is often used in scenarios where hard data is scarce, and decisions are made based on beliefs or expert knowledge.
Classical Assumes that all outcomes in a sample space are equally likely. It is best suited for situations where you can clearly define all possible outcomes, and each outcome has the same chance of occurring, i.e., symmetrical weight.
Empirical Relies on actual data collected from experiments or observations. Empirical probability is calculated by dividing the number of times an event occurs by the total number of trials or observations.

Each approach offers a unique perspective and methodology for determining the likelihood of events.

There are three primary approaches to understanding and calculating probability:

  • Subjective: a doctor might use subjective probability to estimate the likelihood of a patient's recovery based on their experience and the patient's symptoms. Useful when thinking about how to test or experiment something.
  • Classical: An example is tossing a fair coin, where the chances of landing heads or tails are both 50%.
  • Empirical: It is particularly useful in situations where historical data is available, such as calculating the probability of rain based on weather records.

Classical Probability

Probability is a measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. $$ P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of outcomes in event}}{\text{Number of outcomes in sample space}} $$

In the context of mechanical engineering, probability can be used to assess the reliability of systems and components.

For example, if a gear in a transmission system has a 1 in 100 chance of failing under certain conditions, its probability of failure is 0.01.

Empirical Probability

Empirical probability is calculated based on observed data or past experiences. It measures the likelihood of an event by dividing the number of observed occurrences by the total number of observations.

Relative frequency, the ratio of an event's occurrences to total trials, forms the empirical basis for calculating an event's probability.

$$ \text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{\text{Frequency of the event occurring}}{\text{Total number of trials}} $$

Example: Consider a biomechanical experiment where a prosthetic knee is subjected to repeated stress tests. Out of 500 tests, the knee joint fails 5 times. The relative frequency of failure is calculated as: $$ \text{Relative Frequency of Failure} = \frac{5}{500} = 0.01 $$ This means that the probability of failure in a single stress test, based on past data, is 1%.

Empirical Probability: Examples

Space Shuttle Mission Failures

Following the Columbia disaster on February 1, 2003, NASA's second in 113 missions, we estimate the probability of future mission success.

Defining P as probability and P(A) as success probability:

$$ P(A) = \frac{111}{113} = 0.98 $$

This suggests a 0.98 probability of successful completion for future space shuttle missions.

Bolt Production

Example: In a factory, a machine produces bolts. After producing 10,000 bolts, it is observed that 20 are defective. The relative frequency of producing a defective bolt is:

$$ \text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{20}{10,000} = 0.002 $$

This indicates that, based on past production, there is a 0.2% probability of the machine producing a defective bolt.

The Law of Large Numbers states that as the number of trials increases, the relative frequency of an event gets closer to the true probability of the event.

Set Theory

An Introduction To Belonging

Set theory is a fundamental branch of mathematics that deals with the study of sets, which are collections of distinct objects. It forms the basis for much of modern mathematical analysis and provides a framework for understanding concepts such as numbers, relations, functions, and probability. Set theory introduces notions like union, intersection, and complement of sets, as well as the idea of subsets and the universal set. It is used to formalize and clarify the structure of mathematical logic and is essential for understanding more advanced mathematical concepts. In set theory, objects within a set are called elements or members, and the study of how these sets interact and relate to each other is a key focus of the field.

Intersection of Set Theory and Probability

Set theory and probability form the mathematical backbone of analyzing and predicting outcomes in various fields, providing tools to quantify uncertainty and make informed decisions.

  • Set Theory: A fundamental branch of mathematics focused on the study of sets, collections of distinct objects. Set theory underpins probability by defining events as sets and exploring relationships through operations like union, intersection, and complement.
  • Combinatorics: In the context of probability, combinatorics involves the study of counting, arrangement, and combination of elements, crucial for calculating the likelihood of various events and understanding the structure of different outcomes.

Rules In Detail: Special Rule of Addition

The probability of either of two mutually exclusive events occurring is the sum of their individual probabilities.

Formula: $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y)$
Only for mutually exclusive events.

Generalization:
For multiple mutually exclusive events X1, X2, ..., Xn: $P(X1 \cup \ldots \cup Xn) = P(X1) + \ldots + P(Xn)$.

Example:

Consider three mutually exclusive failure scenarios of a machine part:

  • Failure due to overheating (X1) with a probability of 0.1.
  • Failure due to excessive vibration (X2) with a probability of 0.05.
  • Failure due to material degradation by UV exposure (X3) with a probability of 0.08.

Assuming the causes are completely independent of each other, the probability of the part failing due to any of these causes is $$\begin{aligned}P(X1 \cup X2 \cup X3) &= P(X1) + P(X2) + P(X3) \\ &= 0.1 + 0.05 + 0.08 \\ &= 0.23 \end{aligned}$$

Rules In Detail: General Rule of Addition

The general rule of addition helps us calculate the probability of either of two events, X and Y, occurring. This rule is especially important when the events are not mutually exclusive, meaning they can happen together. It adjusts for any overlap between the events.

Formula: $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y) - P(X \cap Y)$

Generalization: This formula can be extended to more than two events by considering all their intersections.

Let's apply this to a practical situation in a mechanical system:

  • Event X: A component fails due to wear and tear with a probability of 0.3.
  • Event Y: The same component fails due to a lubrication issue with a probability of 0.2.
  • It's known that 5% of failures are caused by both wear and tear and lubrication issues.

What is overall probability of a failure due to either cause

A. $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y)$ $\phantom{}=0.3 + 0.2 = 0.50$

B. $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y) - P(X \cap Y)$ $\phantom{}=0.3 + 0.2 - 0.05 = 0.45$

Rules In Detail: Multiplication

Special

General

This rule calculates the probability of two independent events X and Y occurring together. It's the product of their individual probabilities.

Formula: $P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \times P(Y)$ for independent events X and Y.

Consider two independent events in a mechanical system:

  • Event X: A sensor operates nominally with $P(X) = 0.9$.
  • Event Y: A valve operates nominally with $P(Y) = 0.8$.

The probability of both the sensor activating and the valve opening successfully is:

$P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \times P(Y) = 0.9 \times 0.8 = \class{info}{0.72}$.

This rule calculates the probability of two events X and Y occurring together, accounting for the possibility of dependence between them.

Formula: $P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \times P(Y|X)$, where Y is conditional on X.

Consider two events in a mechanical system with a dependent relationship:

  • Event X: A machine starts successfully with $P(X) = 0.85$.
  • Event Y: The cooling system engages with $P(Y|X) = 0.9$, i.e., it starts 90% of the time after the machine starts.

The probability of both the machine starting and the cooling system engaging is:

$P(X \cap Y) = P(X) \times P(Y|X) = 0.85 \times 0.9 = \class{info}{0.765}$.

Multiple Independent Events

Independent Events

If two events, $A$ and $B$, are independent, the probability of both events occurring is the intersection of their individual probabilities.

  • Formula: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$
  • Simply, the probability of both events occurring is the product of their individual probabilities.

Example: Flipping a coin twice

  • Each flip is independent of the last.
  • The probability of getting heads on each flip is $P(H) = \frac{1}{2}$.
  • Since the two flips are independent, the probability of getting heads both times is:
  • $$P(H_1 \cap H_2) = P(H_1) \times P(H_2) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Multiple Independent Events

  • If you have more than 2 independent events, say $A$, $B$, and $C$, and you want the probability that all these events occur, you multiply the probabilities of each event.
  • Formula: $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \times P(B) \times P(C)$

Example: Rolling 3 six-sided dice

  • For a specific outcome, say the dice land on 3,5,4 in no specific order, the process is the same.
  • The probability of rolling a specific number is: $P(\#) = \frac{1}{6}$
  • The probability of rolling 3 dice and getting
    (3, 5, 4) is:

    $$\begin{aligned} P(\text{3,5,4}) &= P(3) \times P(5) \times P(4) \\ &= \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{216} \end{aligned} $$

The probability of two (or more) independent events occurring together is called Joint Probability.

The key thing here is "no particular order", meaning what is the expected probability for multiple events happening totally independent of each other.

We can use the joint probability to determine more complex probabilities.

Probabilty (Definitions)

Probability

The measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. Probability is quantified as a number between \(0\) and \(1\), where \(0\) indicates impossibility and \(1\) indicates certainty.

Sample Space (\(S\))

The set of all possible outcomes of a probabilistic experiment. For example, in a quality test of a car engine, the sample space might include outcomes like 'Pass' and 'Fail'.

Event

A subset of the sample space (\(S\)) that consists of one or more outcomes. An event can be something like 'Engine Fails' in a quality test.

Classical Probability

A probability approach where all outcomes in the sample space are assumed to be equally likely. The probability of an event \(A\) is calculated as: \[ P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of outcomes in event}}{\text{Number of outcomes in sample space}} \]

Empirical Probability

Probability based on observed data or past experiences, calculated by dividing the number of observed occurrences by the total number of observations.

Set Theory

A branch of mathematics focused on the study of sets, collections of distinct objects. Set theory underpins probability by defining events as sets and exploring relationships through operations like union, intersection, and complement.
