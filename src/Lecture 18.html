Geometric Interpretation

Statistic:

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{SS_{between}}{SS_{Within}}= \frac{\partial^2(fit,null)}{\partial^2(fit,data)} $$

Effect Size:

$$R^2=\eta^2=\frac{\partial^2(fit,null)}{\partial^2(data,null)}$$

Large F, distance between fit and null (mean and grand mean: group variance) in euclidean space is large.

Small F, distance between the fit and data (mean and points: residuals) is larger that fit-null distance.

Think back to trigonometry, if the ratio of the opposite over the adjacent is large, then the angle is large.

Adjusting the $SS$ Terms

Statistic:

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{SS_{between}}}{\color{lightblue} {SS_{Within}}}=\frac{\color{orange}{\partial^2(fit,null)}} {\color{lightblue}{\partial^2(fit,data)}}$$

Effect Size:

$$R^2=\frac{\color{orange}{\partial^2(fit,null)}}{\color{seagreen}{\partial^2(data,null)}}$$

Statistic:

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{\partial^2(fit,null)}}{\color{lightblue}{\partial^2(fit,\color{purple}{reduced})}}$$

Effect Size:

$$\eta^2_{p}=\frac{\color{orange}{\partial^2(fit,null)}}{\color{purple}{\partial^2(reduced,null)}}=\frac{SS_{Between}}{SS_{Total}}$$

Impact of Variance

How does the assumption of homogeneity of variances impact the interpretation of ANOVA results?

Discuss what might happen if this assumption is violated, and how might such a violation affect the validity of the ANOVA test conclusions?

Homogeneity of variances (equal variances) across groups is essential for the validity of ANOVA because it ensures the F-ratio remains an accurate measure of group differences. When variances are equal, the pooled variance estimate provides a fair comparison between group means. If this assumption is violated, particularly in cases of unequal sample sizes, ANOVA becomes prone to type I and type II errors. Specifically, the test may falsely detect differences when none exist or fail to detect real differences, especially if variances are higher in groups with smaller sample sizes. Violations can also lead to biased estimates, making the ANOVA conclusions unreliable.

Note: In the case of an unbalanced design (or unequal variances), you can use Welch's ANOVA, or another non-parametric test like the Kruskal-Wallis test or my favorite: bootstrapping!

The F-statistic in ANOVA depends on the assumption of equal variances among groups, known as homogeneity of variances. If the assumption is met, the F-distribution under the null hypothesis accurately represents what we expect under no treatment effect. However, when variances differ significantly across groups, especially if sample sizes vary, it can skew the F-statistic and lead to incorrect conclusions.

In terms of error rates, unequal variances can inflate the type I error rate, where we incorrectly reject the null hypothesis, or type II error rate, where we fail to detect a true effect. This violation introduces bias, as groups with higher variance but smaller sample sizes disproportionately affect the results.

To address variance inequality, we can use alternative approaches like Welch’s ANOVA, which adjusts for unequal variances. Another approach is transforming the data (e.g., using logarithmic or square root transformations) to stabilize variances across groups before running a traditional ANOVA. Additionally, we might consider non-parametric alternatives if the assumption cannot be met, as these tests do not rely on equal variances.

Purpose: By exploring the assumption of homogeneity of variances, students gain insight into why ANOVA requires careful consideration of data characteristics and how to proceed if the assumption fails. This concept also encourages critical thinking about model assumptions and the practical steps for addressing real-world data issues.

Understanding the F-Statistic

How does the F-statistic used in ANOVA compare conceptually to the t and z statistics used in other types of hypothesis testing

Consider how each of these statistics relates to their respective null and sampling distributions, and what they indicate about the data relative to the null hypothesis.

F-statistic

  • Conceptual Use: Used to compare the variances between groups to determine if the means are significantly different across three or more groups.
  • Distribution: Follows an F-distribution, derived from two chi-squared distributions, dependent on two types of degrees of freedom: between and within groups.
  • Indication About Data: A high F-value suggests significant variability between groups compared to within, indicating that not all group means are equal, contrary to the null hypothesis of equal means.

t-statistic

  • Conceptual Use: Used to compare the means between two groups, particularly when the sample sizes are small and the population standard deviation is unknown. Suitable for either two independent samples or two related samples.
  • Distribution: Follows a t-distribution, appropriate when the population standard deviation is estimated from the data. The shape of the t-distribution is dependent on the sample size through its degrees of freedom.
  • Indication About Data: A high t-value suggests a significant difference between the group means, implying a rejection of the null hypothesis if it states the means should be equal.

z-statistic

  • Conceptual Use: Used for hypothesis testing when the population variance is known and the sample size is large. It is typically used to test the difference between a sample mean and a population mean or between two population means when the variances are known.
  • Distribution: Follows a normal distribution, under the assumption of normal data distribution or sufficient sample size for the Central Limit Theorem to apply.
  • Indication About Data: A high z-value indicates that the sample mean is significantly different from the population mean under the null hypothesis, assuming a normal distribution.

Purpose: This question aims to deepen understanding of the F-statistic by comparing it with other more familiar statistics like t and z. It encourages students to think about the roles these statistics play in determining the probability of observing the given data under the null hypothesis.

Choosing Between One-Way and Repeated Measures ANOVA

Under what circumstances might you choose to use repeated measures ANOVA instead of one-way ANOVA

Consider the types of research questions that each method is best suited to address and the data characteristics required for each.

Repeated measures ANOVA is more appropriate when the same subjects are measured multiple times under different conditions. This method accounts for within-subject variability and is more powerful for detecting differences when conditions affect the same subjects. One-way ANOVA is used when different subjects are measured under similar conditions but only once.

Purpose: This question helps students differentiate when to use one-way ANOVA versus repeated measures ANOVA. It prompts them to consider the structure of the dataset, the specific hypotheses being tested, and the appropriate statistical approach based on the research design (e.g., within-subjects vs. between-subjects).

Dealing With Multiple Factors

ANOVA Does Even More

One-Way ANOVA

One-Way ANOVA considers multiple groups belonging to a single categorical family called a factor level, i.e. sandwiches, treatment types, etc.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{SS_{between}}}{\color{lightblue}{SS_{Within}}}$$

One-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA

Just as we discussed with before $\sim$ after in two-group NHST, ANOVA has special methods for dealing with repeatedly measuring from a single subject. It takes the form of:

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\color{orange}{SS_{between}}}{\color{lightblue}{(SS_{Within}- SS_{random})}}=\frac{SS_{between.timepoints}}{SS_{fixed}}$$

N-Way ANOVA

We aren't limited to one level (plus time) and can model multiple factors, though it starts getting complicated because we have new kinds of terms to discuss called interactions. Note that each new factor level will get its own $\mathcal{F}$-statistic.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\{ SS_{b.group1},\, SS_{b.group2},\, \ldots ,\, SS_{b.groupN},\, \{ SS_{\color{orange}{interactions}}\} \} } {SS_{Within}}$$

N-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA

Just like in the One-Way approach, we now have to consider the effects of time and remove the random effects, i.e. the within subject variation.

$$\mathcal{F}=\frac{\{ SS_{b.time},\, SS_{b.group2},\, \ldots ,\, SS_{b.groupN},\, \{ SS_{\color{orange}{interactions}}\} \} } {SS_{Within}-SS_{random}}$$

Note: As we add more and more factors, we stop calling it ANOVA and just say "Factor Analysis", and it starts taking on a different purpose.

Namely, Factor Analysis is a way of reducing data dimensionality by merging variables with highly explained variance.

Two-Way ANOVA

A two-way ANOVA allows us to investigate the (individual & combined) effects of two factors (explanatory/independent variables) on a quantitative variable (response).

In our memorization performance and noise level experiment, we could for example investigate if gender (or the combine effect of gender and noise) also affects our response variable.

The results from a two way ANOVA will give information about the main effect and the interaction effect:

  • Main effect of factor 1 (e.g.: noise ... this factor has 4 levels)
  • Main effect of factor 2 (e.g.: gender ... this factor has 2 levels)
  • Effect due to the combination/interaction of factor 1 and factor 2 (e.g.: does the effect of the noise depends on the gender too)

Two-Way ANOVA

We can think of two-way ANOVA as a nesting of one-way ANOVAs.
That is, we end up with, essentially, three one-way ANOVAs:

  • one for each factor
  • and one for the combined factors

The computation of the $SS$ terms for each factor and the error terms are virtually similar than for a one-way ANOVA.

The interaction variability refers to the degree to which the combined effect of two independent variables on the dependent variable differs from what would be expected based on their individual effects alone.

Two-Way ANOVA

We can think of Two-Way ANOVA as a nesting of One-Way ANOVAs. That is, we end up with, essentially, three One-Way ANOVAs: one for each factor-level and one for the combined factor levels.

For factor-levels ($p= \{A,B\}$) $A$ and $B$: $$\left\{ \mathcal{F}_A = \frac{\class{success}{MS_{A}}}{MS_{E}}, \mathcal{F}_B = \frac{\class{info}{MS_{B}}}{MS_{E}}, \mathcal{F}_{A*B} = \frac{MS_{\class{success}{A}*\class{info}{B}}}{MS_{E}}\quad \right\}$$

Variability Between A-Level Factors

$$SS_A = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} n_{i\cdot} \left( \bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot} - \hat{y} \right)^2,\quad {\scriptstyle \mathrm{dof}=n_a - 1}$$

Variability Between B-Level Factors

$$SS_B = \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} n_{\cdot j} \left( \bar{y}_{\cdot j \cdot} - \hat{y} \right)^2,\quad {\scriptstyle \mathrm{dof}=n_B - 1}$$

Variability Within groupings (Error)

$$SS_E = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} \sum_{k=1}^{n_{ij}} \left( y_{ijk} - \bar{y}_{ij\cdot} \right)^2,\quad {\scriptstyle \mathrm{dof}= N - (n_a + n_b - 1 + (n_a - 1)(n_b - 1))}$$

$n_a$: number of levels for Factor A; $n_b$: number of levels for Factor B; $n_{ij}$: number of observations for each combination of levels $(i, j)$; $\hat{y}$: grand mean; $\bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot}$: weighted mean for Factor A; $\bar{y}_{\cdot j \cdot}$: weighted mean for Factor B; $\bar{y}_{ij \cdot}$: mean for each combination of levels $(i, j)$; $y_{ijk}$: individual observation in combination $(i, j)$; $df$: degrees of freedom; $N$: total number of observations.

  • Total Sum of Squares (SSTotal): Measures the total variability in the data by comparing each observation to the overall grand mean.
  • Sum of Squares for Factor A (SSA): Quantifies the variability due to differences between levels of Factor A, weighted by the number of observations per level.
  • Sum of Squares for Factor B (SSB): Represents the variability due to differences between levels of Factor B, also weighted by the observations in each level.
  • Sum of Squares for the Interaction (SSAB): Captures the variability from the combined effect of Factors A and B, using cell means adjusted by Factor A and Factor B levels.
  • Sum of Squares for Error (SSE): Represents the residual variability within each cell that is not explained by Factors A, B, or their interaction.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Factor A (dfA): Calculated as the number of levels in Factor A minus one, representing independent information for Factor A.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Factor B (dfB): Calculated as the number of levels in Factor B minus one, representing independent information for Factor B.
  • Degrees of Freedom for the Interaction (dfAB): The product of the degrees of freedom for Factor A and Factor B, accounting for their combined effect.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Error (dfE): Calculated as the total number of observations minus the degrees of freedom used by Factors A, B, and their interaction.
  • Total Degrees of Freedom (dfTotal): Represents the total independent information in the dataset, calculated as the total number of observations minus one.

Two-Way ANOVA

The interaction term, $SS_{A*B}$, takes into account the fact that a measurement belongs to both $A$ and $B$ factors, which have some effect, $\epsilon$, that needs to be accounted for.

$\epsilon$ is the sum of each factor-level effects.

Factor-level effects are actually the residuals of the between groups variances.

Like other $SS$ terms, $SS_{A\cdot B}$ is the residual of the true grouping minus the sum of the factor-level effects.

$$ \begin{aligned} \epsilon &=& \epsilon_A + \epsilon_B, \\ \epsilon_i &=& (\bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot} - \hat{y}), \\ \therefore \epsilon_{A_i} &=& (\bar{y}_{\{A \mid i \in A\}} - \hat{y}) \end{aligned} $$
$$SS_{A \cdot B} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} n_{ij} \left( (\bar{y}_{ij \cdot} - \hat{y}) - (\epsilon_{A_i} + \epsilon_{B_j}) \right)^2$$

With substitution, our interaction term becomes:

$$SS_{A \cdot B} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} n_{ij} \left( (\bar{y}_{ij \cdot} - \hat{y}) - ((\bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot} - \hat{y}) + (\bar{y}_{\cdot j \cdot} - \hat{y})) \right)^2$$

Which simplifies to:

$$SS_{A \cdot B} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} n_{ij} \left( \bar{y}_{ij \cdot} - \bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j \cdot} + \hat{y} \right)^2$$

To match the desired form, we write:

$$SS_{A \cdot B} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_a} \sum_{j=1}^{n_b} n_{ij} \left( \bar{y}_{ij \cdot} - (\bar{y}_{i \cdot \cdot} + \bar{y}_{\cdot j \cdot} - \hat{y}) \right)^2$$
  • Total Sum of Squares (SSTotal): Measures the total variability in the data by comparing each observation to the overall grand mean.
  • Sum of Squares for Factor A (SSA): Quantifies the variability due to differences between levels of Factor A, weighted by the number of observations per level.
  • Sum of Squares for Factor B (SSB): Represents the variability due to differences between levels of Factor B, also weighted by the observations in each level.
  • Sum of Squares for the Interaction (SSAB): Captures the variability from the combined effect of Factors A and B, using cell means adjusted by Factor A and Factor B levels.
  • Sum of Squares for Error (SSE): Represents the residual variability within each cell that is not explained by Factors A, B, or their interaction.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Factor A (dfA): Calculated as the number of levels in Factor A minus one, representing independent information for Factor A.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Factor B (dfB): Calculated as the number of levels in Factor B minus one, representing independent information for Factor B.
  • Degrees of Freedom for the Interaction (dfAB): The product of the degrees of freedom for Factor A and Factor B, accounting for their combined effect.
  • Degrees of Freedom for Error (dfE): Calculated as the total number of observations minus the degrees of freedom used by Factors A, B, and their interaction.
