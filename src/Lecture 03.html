Probability And Set Theory

Recall, we covered several definitions, today we will dive in deeper.

Approaches

Probability theory is a fundamental part of mathematics and statistics that deals with the likelihood of different outcomes in various scenarios. There are three primary approaches to understanding and calculating probability:

Approach Description
Subjective Based on personal judgment and available information. It is often used in scenarios where hard data is scarce, and decisions are made based on beliefs or expert knowledge.
Classical Assumes that all outcomes in a sample space are equally likely. It is best suited for situations where you can clearly define all possible outcomes, and each outcome has the same chance of occurring, i.e., symmetrical weight.
Empirical Relies on actual data collected from experiments or observations. Empirical probability is calculated by dividing the number of times an event occurs by the total number of trials or observations.

Each approach offers a unique perspective and methodology for determining the likelihood of events.

There are three primary approaches to understanding and calculating probability:

  • Subjective: a doctor might use subjective probability to estimate the likelihood of a patient's recovery based on their experience and the patient's symptoms. Useful when thinking about how to test or experiment something.
  • Classical: An example is tossing a fair coin, where the chances of landing heads or tails are both 50%.
  • Empirical: It is particularly useful in situations where historical data is available, such as calculating the probability of rain based on weather records.

Intersection of Set Theory and Probability

Set theory and probability form the mathematical backbone of analyzing and predicting outcomes in various fields, providing tools to quantify uncertainty and make informed decisions.

  • Set Theory: A fundamental branch of mathematics focused on the study of sets, collections of distinct objects. Set theory underpins probability by defining events as sets and exploring relationships through operations like union, intersection, and complement.
  • Combinatorics: In the context of probability, combinatorics involves the study of counting, arrangement, and combination of elements, crucial for calculating the likelihood of various events and understanding the structure of different outcomes.

Rules In Detail: Union

The union of two events $A$ and $B$ ($A \cup B$) represents the event that either event or both events occur.

For Non-Mutually exclusive events: $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y) - \class{info}{A \cap B}$.

For mutually exclusive events: $P(X \cup Y) = P(X) + P(Y)$.

Example:

Consider rolling a single 6-sided die twice:

  • Let $A$ be the event the first roll shows a 2
  • Let $B$ be the event the second roll shows a 5

Since the one die cannot show both a 2 and 5 simultaneously, $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive: $P(A\cup B) = P(A) + P(B) = \frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$.

Consider rolling two 6-sided dice:

  • Let $A$ be the event the sum of the roll is 7
  • Let $B$ be the event at least one face shows a 4

This is more complex, $P(A)$ is the union of all possible ways the 2 dice can add up to 7, while $P(B)$ is the marginal probability of one die showing a 4.

Multiple Independent Events

Union of Independent Events

If two events, $A$ and $B$, are independent, the probability that either event occurs is the union of their individual probabilities.

  • Formula: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
  • Simply, the probability of either event occurring is the sum of their probabilities, minus the probability of both occurring.

Example: Rolling a six-sided die

  • Let $A$ be the event of rolling a 3, and
  • $B$ be the event of rolling a 5

The probability of rolling a 3 is $P(A) = \frac{1}{6}$.

The probability of rolling a 5 is $P(B) = \frac{1}{6}$.

Since these events are mutually exclusive (you can't roll a 3 and a 5 at the same time), the union is simply: $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$$

Intersection of Independent Events

If two events, $A$ and $B$, are independent, the probability of both events occurring is the intersection of their individual probabilities.

  • Formula: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$
  • Simply, the probability of both events occurring together is the product of their individual probabilities.

Example: Rolling two six-sided dice

  • Let $A$ be the event that the first die shows a 4, and
  • $B$ be the event that the second die shows a 6.

Each roll is independent of the other.

The probability of rolling a 4 on the first die is $P(A) = \frac{1}{6}$.

The probability of rolling a 6 on the second die is $P(B) = \frac{1}{6}$.

The probability of both events happening together is: $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$$

The probability of two (or more) independent events occurring together is called Joint Probability.

The union of events is used when we want to find the probability that at least one of the events occurs.

The intersection of events is used when we want to find the probability that both events occur simultaneously.

Joint probability helps us understand the likelihood of multiple independent events happening together, which can be crucial in complex probability scenarios.

Let's revisit the sum of 2 dice question.

Rules In Detail: Intersection

The intersection of two events $A$ and $B$ ($A \cap B$) represents the probability that both events occur simultaneously.

For Independent events: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$.

For dependent events: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B \mid A)$.

Example:

Consider rolling two 6-sided dice:

  • Let $A$ be the event that the first die shows a 4
  • Let $B$ be the event that the second die shows a 6

Since the outcome of one die does not affect the other, $A$ and $B$ are independent: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$.

Consider drawing cards without replacement:

  • Let $A$ be the event of drawing a King on the first draw
  • Let $B$ be the event of drawing a Queen on the second draw

These events are dependent, as drawing a King affects the probability of drawing a Queen next: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B \mid A) = \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{4}{51}$.

When events are dependent, the intersection is directly tied to conditional probability: $P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$.

The intersection is the probability that both events happen at the same time.

For independent events, simply multiply the probabilities.

For dependent events, you need to use conditional probability.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring, given that another event has already occurred.

It allows us to adjust our probability estimates based on new information.

The probability of event \(A\) given event \(B\) is: $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)},$$ where \(P(B) > 0\) is the probability of event B.

Suppose we roll two dice. Let \(A\) be the event the sum of the dice is 7, and let \(B\) be the event the first die shows a 3.

What is the probability the sum of the dice is 7 given the first die is showing 3

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$
$$\phantom{}= \frac{P(A) \times P(B)}{P(B)}$$
$$\phantom{}= \frac{(1/6)(1/6)}{1/6}$$

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{1}{6}$$

  • Conditional probability reflects how the probability of \(A\) changes in the context of \(B\) having happened.
  • Reduction of Sample Space: The occurrence of \(B\) reduces the sample space to only those outcomes where \(B\) happens.

Application:

Conditional probability is fundamental in scenarios such as:

  • Medical Testing: Estimating the probability of having a disease given a positive test result.
  • Reliability Engineering: Calculating the likelihood of system failure given that a component has failed.
  • Finance: Assessing the probability of a market event given recent economic indicators.
  • Visual: A probability tree diagram showing how conditional probabilities are calculated or a Venn diagram highlighting the relationship between events \(A\) and \(B\).

"Chain Rule" of Probability

A tool that calculates the probability of the intersection of events or the joint distribution of random variables using conditional probabilities.

It enables the calculation of joint probabilities by sequentially considering the probability of each event in the context of previous events.

For two events \(A\) and \(B\), the chain rule states: $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)$$

  • The chain rule breaks down complex problems into simpler steps by leveraging conditional probabilities.
  • Sequential Analysis: The probability of each event is calculated based on the occurrence of previous events.
  • Be cautious when applying the chain rule to ensure the correct order of conditional probabilities.

Application:

The chain rule is essential for:

  • Bayesian Inference: Updating the probability of a hypothesis as more evidence or data becomes available.
  • Risk Assessment: Calculating the likelihood of a sequence of events leading to a system failure.
  • Machine Learning: Decomposing complex joint distributions in probabilistic models.
  • Visual: The image shows how the chain rule applies to a sequence of events, making it easier to understand the dependencies among events.

Law of Total Probability

The law of total probability allows us to calculate the probability of an event by considering all possible ways that the event can occur, taking into account different conditions.

It provides a way to compute probabilities when the event can be triggered by multiple distinct scenarios.

Let \(B_1, B_2, \dots, B_n\) be a partition of the sample space, meaning that the \(B_i\)s are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. For any event \(A\), the law states: $$P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \mid B_i) \cdot P(B_i)$$

  • The law of total probability is essential when events can occur due to several distinct, mutually exclusive conditions.
  • Comprehensive Analysis: This law helps in breaking down complex probability problems into manageable parts.
  • Ensure that the events \(B_i\) form a complete partition of the sample space; otherwise, the law of total probability does not apply.

Application:

The law of total probability is particularly useful for:

  • Medical Diagnosis: Estimating the overall probability of a disease considering different symptoms.
  • Reliability Engineering: Determining the probability of system success by considering the success of individual components.
  • Risk Management: Calculating the probability of a risk materializing from various sources or causes.
  • Visual: The image illustrates how different scenarios contribute to the total probability of an event occurring.

Combinatorics

The study of counting, arrangement, and combination of objects.

In combinatorics, the Multiplication Principle states that if there are $n_1$ ways to perform one action, $n_2$ ways to perform a second action, and so forth up to $n_k$ ways to perform the $k^{th}$ action, then the total number of ways to perform all actions in sequence is $n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot n_k$.

  • It's crucial in fields like computer science, mathematics, and engineering, helping to solve complex problems involving discrete elements.
  • Combinatorics principles are used to calculate probabilities, optimize solutions, and understand the structure of complex systems.

Example: In a biomechanical lab, three sets of components are tested: Joints ($A_1$), Sensors ($A_2$), and Actuators ($A_3$).

Sets $A_1$, $A_2$, and $A_3$ contain, respectively, $n_1 = 3$, $n_2 = 4$, and $n_3 = 5$ elements.

To test all possible combinations of Joints, Sensors, and Actuators, there are $$n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3 = 3 \cdot 4 \cdot 5 = 60$$ unique configurations.

Permutations

Permutations refer to the different ways in which a set of objects can be arranged or ordered.

Example:
Consider a set of three letters $\{A, B, C\}$, the possible permutations of the whole set are ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, and CBA.

Formula:
The number of permutations of $n$ objects is given by $n!$, where $n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \ldots \times 1$.
So, for our example, $n! = 3! = 3\cdot 2\cdot 1 = \class{black}{6}$

When arranging a subset of $r$ elements from a set of $n$ elements, the number of permutations is given by $^n\!P_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$.

Notice, this is the total ($n!$) scaled by the number of permutations of the groups ($(n-r)!$).

Example:
 From a group of 5 different books, the number of ways to arrange any 3 books is calculated as $$\require{cancel}\phantom{}^5\!P_3 = \frac{5!}{(5-3)!} = \frac{5 \times 4 \times 3 \times \cancel{2 \times 1}}{\cancel{2 \times 1}} = 60\text{ ways}.$$

Understanding permutations is essential in experimental design, allowing researchers to systematically explore all possible arrangements of variables or conditions.

Combinations

Combinations refer to the selection of objects from a set where the order of selection does not matter.

Combinations:

The sequence in which the elements are selected does not affect the outcome. In other words, when you select a group of items, all arrangements of those items are considered the same combination.

Selecting $r$ objects from $n$ distinct objects:
Formula: $^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$
Example: Choosing 3 of 20 lab assistants: $$^{20}C_3 = \frac{20!}{3!(20-3)!} = 1,140\text{ ways.}$$

Combination of Combinations: This concept involves calculating combinations for multiple independent selections within a single experiment or scenario.

Example: In a research lab, the director needs to select team members for a project.

Chemists: From seven chemist applicants, two must be chosen. The number of ways to select 2 chemists from 7 is $^{7}C_2 = 21$ ways.

Physicists: From nine physicist applicants, three must be chosen. The number of ways to select 3 physicists from 9 is $^{9}C_3 = 84$ ways.

Total Selections: To determine the total number of ways to choose both the chemists and the physicists, multiply their individual combinations: $21 \times 84 = 1,764$ ways.

Combinations are fundamental in statistical analysis and probability theory, enabling the understanding of different groupings without considering the order of elements.

Random Variables

A random variable $X$ is a function that maps outcomes from a sample space $\Omega$ to real numbers.

Mathematically, we write this as:

$$X: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$

There are two types:

Discrete Random Variables

Discrete random variables measure outcomes at distinct or finite points.

Examples include the count of defects in a product or errors in data transmission.

Continuous Random Variables

Continuous random variables can assume any value within a range of real numbers.

Examples include temperature readings, electrical current, or elapsed time.

Understanding random variables is crucial for analyzing data and making predictions in various fields.

In probability theory, a random variable is a fundamental concept that helps us quantify uncertain outcomes. A random variable is essentially a function that assigns numerical values to each outcome in a sample space. This allows us to work mathematically with probabilities and outcomes, converting qualitative events into quantitative analysis.

Random Variables

The expectation (or expected value) of a random variable is a measure of the central tendency of its distribution.

For a random variable \( X \) with discrete outcomes \( x_i \) and corresponding probabilities \( P(X = x_i) \), the expectation is given by:

$$E[X] = \sum_{i=1} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$

Consider a random variable with 2 equal bins: Bin $1\rightarrow 0$, Bin $2 \rightarrow 1$.

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 0 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \)

Now consider a scenario with 7 bins: mapped to 0 through 6 values

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 4 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 5 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 6 \cdot \frac{1}{7} \)

Simplifying:

\( E[X] = \frac{1}{7} (0 + 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) = \frac{1}{7} \cdot 21 = 3 \)

How would the expectation change if the bins were unequal sizes

Expectation is defined as the weighted sum of all possible values, where the weights are the probabilities of those values occurring.
