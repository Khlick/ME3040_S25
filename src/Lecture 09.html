Confidence Intervals

The Estimation Of Uncertainty

We discussed confidence intervals before.

Standard Error

The standard error may be thought of as a 'typical' distance between the sample statistics and the population parameter.

To calculate standard error of the mean, we fist calculate the standard deviation of the sample.

Then we divide by $\sqrt{n}$ (where $n$ is our sample size.)

$$SE=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$

$SE$ estimates the spread of our sampling distribution!

Note: The variability of a sample statistic, called the standard error (SE), is distinct yet related to the standard deviation of sample values.

Recall: standard deviation is the typical distance between the sample and the sample statistic.

It turns out, if our sample size (for one experiment) is large enough, we can estimate how far off we might be, or rather how UNCERTAIN we are about our sample statistic (mean) representing the population parameter (mu).

With this information, we can put a number to how uncertain we are.

Considering Sample Size

Sample Size Matters!
Increasing sample size reduces the variability of sample statistics, making them more accurate (narrower sampling distribution).

Larger sample sizes lead to reduced uncertainty and narrower confidence intervals.

If the sampling distribution in narrower, what also happens to the standard Error? 95% Confidence Interval?

Think about SE and CI calculations. sigma smaller or n larger (both too), then 1.96*se also smaller.

Confidence Intervals

The sampling distribution of a statistic, normally distributed around the population parameter, allows us to quantify the likelihood of a sample statistic's proximity to the true population parameter.

For example, 95% of our statistics fall within $\pm 1.96\times SE$ of the parameter, indicating a 95% probability that the true parameter lies within this range from any observed statistic.

This range is termed a confidence interval.

Confidence intervals quantify the uncertainty associated with estimating the parameter.

Uncertainty is expressed in "confidence intervals". It acknowledges that the exact value of the parameter (such as the mean or proportion) is unknown and might not be precisely determined from a sample.

While we typically will use 95% for our confidence intervals, it is common to see other intervals, such as 90 or 99%.

What is the probability my observed statistic came from this theoretical distribution

I mentioned before, we have multiple ways of estimating a population parameter.

In some cases, it will be clear what the population is, and others, not so much.

NHST: One Sample

In a one-sample (of size N) null hypothesis test, we aim to determine if a sample could have originated from a population with a specific parameter value.

For categorical samples, the comparison is typically against a null chance model with a known success rate, e.g., Binomial Distribution.

In quantitative samples, a reliable null sampling distribution requires a specific model or strong assumptions.

Recall: We selected the binomial model, constructed the expectations as a distribution based on our observation, i.e., the if I saw it by random chance, then counted up the proportion of by-chance observations at least as extreme as our observation.

For a continuous variable, we took our sample and some knowledge about the population (mu, sigma), converted the statistic's sampling distribution to the standard normal (Z) distribution, and we built the population parameter's sampling distribution with a by-chance model (Also Z), so they're on the same scale. Then, we summed the proportion of by-chance observations at least as extreme as our sample's statistic.

How did we determine the direction in which we summed the proportions?

Tailedness in Hypothesis Testing

Determining effects in one direction () or both ()!

One-Tailed Test

Used when we're interested if the test statistic ($\delta$) is significantly greater than or less than the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$).

It specifies the direction of the effect:

  • Right-tailed test: $\delta \gg H_0$.
  • Left-tailed test: $\delta \ll H_0$.

Two-Tailed Test

Used when we're interested in whether the test statistic ($\delta$) is different from $H_0$, without specifying the direction of the difference.

Us it to test for any significant difference.

Mean Calculation

Calculate the mean of the dataset [16 18 16 12 4].

The mean $\bar{x}$ is calculated as:
\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} = \frac{16 + 18 + 16 + 12 + 4}{5} = \frac{66}{5} = 13.20 \]

Permutation Calculation

Given 19 different components, how many ways can you select 4 for a prototype if the order matters?

The number of ways to select 4 components out of 19 with order mattering is given by the permutation formula:
\[ P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} = \frac{19!}{(19-4)!} = \frac{19!}{15!} = 93024 \]

Z-Score Calculation

An alloy's strength is tested with a sample mean of 324 MPa, a standard deviation of 23 MPa, and a sample size of 30. Calculate the z-score for a population mean of 282 MPa.

The z-score is calculated as:
\[ z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} = \frac{324 - 282}{\frac{23}{\sqrt{30}}} \approx 10.00 \]
This indicates the sample mean is 10.00 standard deviations above the population mean.

Binomial Probability

Calculate the probability of getting exactly 4 defective items in a batch of 18, where the probability of a defect is 0.04.

The probability is given by the binomial formula:
\[ P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} = \binom{18}{4} (0.04)^{4} (1 - 0.04)^{14} \]
\[ = 0.004 \]
So, the probability of finding exactly 4 defective items is approximately 0.004.

Confidence Interval

For a sample with a mean of 100, a standard deviation of 15, and a sample size of 35, calculate the 95% confidence interval for the population mean.

The formula for the confidence interval is:
\[ \bar{x} \pm z \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
For a 95% confidence level, the $z$-value is approximately $1.96$.
\[ 100 \pm 1.96 \frac{15}{\sqrt{35}} \approx 100 \pm 4.97 \]
Therefore, the 95% confidence interval is approximately $ 95.03 $ to $ 104.97 $.

Expected Value

Calculate the expected value $E(X)$ for a fair 12-sided die, where each side has an equal chance of landing and the sides are numbered from 1 to 12.

The expected value $E(X)$ for a fair die is given by:
\[ E(X) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{x=1}^{n} x \]
\[ E(X) = \frac{12 + 1}{2} = 6.50 \]

Understanding Quantiles

Quantiles are values that mark where specific proportions of your data fall below.

The quantile function is generally defined as: $\class{success}{Q_{i}(p) = (1 - \gamma)x_{j} + \gamma x_{j+1}}$,
where $j = \lfloor k \rfloor$ for $k = np + m$ and $\gamma = k - j$.

For quantile method 8 (unbiased median), the quantile function is defined as
\(Q_{8}(p) = (1 - \gamma)x_{j} + \gamma x_{j+1}\),
where \(j=\lfloor k \rfloor\), \(m = \frac{p + 1}{3}\), \(k = np + m\), and \(\gamma = k - j\).

Reference: Hyndman and Fan (1996) for a detailed explanation on quantile types and their applications.

Given dataset: \([-0.977, -0.151, -0.103, 0.4, 0.411, 0.95, 0.979, 1.764, 1.868, 2.241]\)

Calculating the median from $Q_8(\class{danger}{0.5})$:

\(Q_{8}(\class{danger}{0.5}) = (1 - \class{pop}{\gamma}) x_{\class{info}{5}} + \class{pop}{\gamma}x_{\class{info}{5}+1} = (1-\class{pop}{0.5}) 0.411 + (\class{pop}{0.5})0.95 = 0.6805\)

Calculating the median from taking mean of middle 2 elements:
$ m = \frac{0.411 + 0.95}{2} = 0.6805$

  • \(n = 10\)
  • \(p = \class{danger}{0.5}\)
  • \(m = \frac{p + 1}{3} = \frac{0.50 + 1}{3} = \frac{1.5}{3} = 0.5\)
  • \(k = np + m = 10 \times 0.50 + 0.5 = 5.5\)
  • \(j = \lfloor k \rfloor = \lfloor 5.5 \rfloor = \class{info}{5}\)
  • \(\gamma = k - j = 5.5 - 5 = \class{pop}{0.5}\)

Quantiles are essential in understanding the distribution of data.

There are several methods or types for quantile calculation, we will use type 8, an unbiased median estimator

What is the data space (units) of the quantile function? The same as the data. That is, we get a location in the dataset where the pth fraction of the number of data points falls below.

How does this relate to the normal distribution? We can use a similar approach and map our data points to the cdf values of the normal distribution. To do that, we need another function.

Interquartile Range

The median divides the data into two equal halves (it is the $50^{th}$ percentile). If we divide each of those halves again, we obtain two additional statistics known as the first (Q1) and third (Q3) quartiles, which are the $25^{th}$ and $75^{th}$ percentiles.

Interquartile range: $\textrm{IQR} = Q3 - Q1$

A value is considered an outlier if it is:

Smaller than $Q1-1.5\times IQR$

or

Larger than $Q3+1.5\times IQR$

MATLAB Code



data = [-0.977, -0.151, -0.103, 0.4, 0.411, 0.95, 0.979, 1.764, 1.868, 2.241];

Q1 = quantile(data, 0.25, 'method', 8);

Q3 = quantile(data, 0.75, 'method', 8);

IQR = Q3 - Q1;

outliers = data(data < Q1 - 1.5 * IQR | data > Q3 + 1.5 * IQR);

disp(outliers);

To objectively identify outliers in a dataset, we consider measures of location and spread to determine when a value significantly deviates from typical cases. A common approach involves using quartiles and the interquartile range (IQR). Typically, most data points fall within approximately 1.5 times the IQR from the quartiles. This method helps in assessing whether a value, such as a notably high lifespan, is an outlier by comparing it against this standard.

Why do we care about summaries of the data? There are other kinds of ranges.
